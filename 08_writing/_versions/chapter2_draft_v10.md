# Chapter 2: Literature Review

## 2.1 Foundations, Scope, and Thesis Positioning

Music streaming puts millions of tracks in front of listeners who have neither the time nor the tools to evaluate them directly. Recommender systems emerged to help, and a substantial body of research has since built up around how to estimate what a user is likely to value from sparse, incomplete evidence [@adomavicius_toward_2005; @lu_recommender_2015; @roy_systematic_2022]. It is more useful to frame this as utility estimation under uncertainty than as preference prediction, because that framing makes explicit what recommendation behaviour actually depends on: the type of evidence collected, how users and items are represented, and the objective used to measure success.

One early implication worth drawing out: listening history is implicit preference evidence, not explicit preference. Play counts and session logs reflect attention and engagement, but they are not the same as a stated choice [@adomavicius_toward_2005; @roy_systematic_2022]. Acting on that evidence still requires deliberate choices about how to aggregate it, what to filter out, and how to account for what it cannot tell you.

This thesis is not trying to propose a new recommendation model or beat a benchmark. The goal is to build a playlist-generation pipeline that is transparent, controllable, observable, and reproducible, within BSc-level constraints. The distinction matters because recommender evaluation is quite sensitive to how experiments are set up — preprocessing decisions, metric framing, and protocol choices all affect results significantly, and strong performance numbers under one setup do not reliably transfer to a design context where inspectable engineering is the primary contribution [@herlocker_evaluating_2004; @ferrari_dacrema_troubling_2021; @anelli_elliot_2021; @bauer_exploring_2024]. Architecture decisions should follow those contribution goals, not benchmark tables [@jannach_measuring_2019].

The chapter moves through a narrowing argument: general recommender trade-offs first, then transparency and evaluation concerns, then preference evidence and profile construction, music-specific challenges, the rationale for a deterministic design, and finally cross-source governance. The purpose is to reduce design uncertainty for Chapter 3, not to survey the field exhaustively.

## 2.2 Core Recommendation Paradigms and Their Trade-offs

Recommender systems are broadly grouped into content-based, collaborative, and hybrid families [@adomavicius_toward_2005; @lu_recommender_2015; @cano_hybrid_2017]. The taxonomy is a useful starting point, but no family is categorically best. What matters is what data is available, how much complexity is justified, and — for a project like this one — whether the design needs to support explanation or user control.

Content-based methods work from item attributes: in music settings, those are metadata, tags, lyrics, and audio descriptors. For a transparency-focused project the practical advantage is that when ranking depends on explicit features, the reasoning behind any recommendation can usually be traced back to those same features [@deldjoo_content-driven_2024; @bogdanov_semantic_2013]. Collaborative filtering takes a different route, inferring relevance from patterns across users and items. That can work well with dense interaction histories, but interpretability tends to weaken when rankings are encoded in latent relationships rather than named feature comparisons [@adomavicius_toward_2005; @zhang_explainable_2020]. Hybrid systems can combine the strengths of both, but the combination adds reasoning complexity that makes audit and explanation harder unless interpretability is deliberately engineered in from the start [@cano_hybrid_2017].

Similarity modelling sits at the centre of these trade-offs. Distance metric, normalization approach, and threshold settings all shape how candidates are ranked — these are real design decisions, not implementation details [@fkih_similarity_2022]. Left implicit they make inspection difficult; made explicit they can be tested and challenged.

There is also a data-conditions argument. Collaborative and hybrid methods tend to rely on denser interaction logs, whereas feature-based approaches can remain workable when those logs are sparse, uneven, or partially unmatched. In a cross-source pipeline, that kind of unevenness is expected rather than exceptional. Under those conditions a theoretically stronger model may still be harder to defend than a simpler one whose inputs and failures can all be straightforwardly audited.

For Chapter 3, this means feature selection, metric choice, normalization, and weighting should be treated as explicit, reviewable commitments rather than defaults set in implementation and quietly left in place.

## 2.3 Transparency, Explainability, Controllability, Observability, and Evaluation

A recurring theme in explainable recommender research is that predictive quality is not enough on its own — users and evaluators often need to understand and question what a system is doing [@tintarev_survey_2007; @tintarev_evaluating_2012; @zhang_explainable_2020]. Four terms are used throughout this thesis and it is worth keeping them separate. Transparency means visibility of recommendation logic. Explainability means understandable reasons for specific outputs. Controllability means the user can influence behaviour through explicit parameters or inputs [@tintarev_survey_2007; @balog_transparent_2019; @jin_effects_2020]. Observability, in this project's specific sense, means run-level visibility — execution state, intermediate diagnostics, and output traces captured well enough to support later inspection.

The distinctions matter because these goals can pull against each other. Research consistently shows that explanations may raise trust or perceived satisfaction without improving genuine understanding [@tintarev_evaluating_2012; @nauta_anecdotal_2023]. Post-hoc explanations can sound plausible while remaining only loosely connected to the mechanism that actually produced the ranking [@zhang_explainable_2020]. For a thesis that foregrounds transparency, that is a real risk rather than an abstract one. Explanations need to stay close to the actual scoring and rule-application process, not be constructed after the fact. Usability research adds a separate pressure: even a technically faithful explanation needs to be comprehensible to a non-expert audience [@tsai_explaining_2018; @knijnenburg_explaining_2012; @afroogh_trust_2024].

Evaluation has to reflect this. Claiming controllability is not enough — changing a control needs to produce interpretable downstream effects, and those effects need to be tested [@jin_effects_2020; @nauta_anecdotal_2023]. Claiming transparency means explanation values should trace back to actual score components. Claiming observability and reproducibility means runs need to be documented through recorded configuration and stage-level diagnostics, not just described in prose [@beel_towards_2016; @bellogin_improving_2021; @cavenaghi_systematic_2023; @anelli_elliot_2021].

The evaluation frame that follows from all this is process-plus-outcome. Process evidence asks whether the pipeline behaved as designed at each stage: were alignment paths recorded, did candidate thresholds shift pool size as expected, can score traces be reconstructed, were playlist assembly rules actually enforced? Outcome evidence asks whether the outputs changed in interpretable ways when something was deliberately varied: did added influence tracks move the ranked list, did changing a feature weight alter score composition in the expected direction, does replaying the same run with the same inputs reproduce the same playlist? This frame suits the thesis scope because it does not require large-scale user studies to produce defensible evidence, but it does demand systematic documentation of explanation fidelity, parameter sensitivity, rule compliance, and replayability. For Chapter 3, that means mechanism-linked explanations, explicit controls, and run-level diagnostics need to be built in from the outset, not retrofitted when there is nothing left to actually test.

## 2.4 Preference Evidence, Profile Construction, and Candidate Shaping

Profile construction tends to get treated as a data-preparation step. It is more than that — it is where every subsequent ranking decision gets its starting conditions, and it deserves first-class treatment in the design.

This project relies on imported listening history, which provides implicit preference evidence rather than a direct statement of what the user wants [@adomavicius_toward_2005; @roy_systematic_2022]. Because of that ambiguity, building a user profile always involves interpretation. The defensible approach is to aggregate the evidence through explicit, reviewable rules rather than treating play counts as objective ground truth.

Content-based music research supports this. Bogdanov et al. show that preference representations can be built from explicit preference examples using semantic audio descriptors, producing profiles that remain inspectable [@bogdanov_semantic_2013]. Deldjoo et al. frame content-driven systems around explicit content layers and note ongoing challenges in transparency, sequence modelling, and efficiency [@deldjoo_content-driven_2024]. Together these point toward a preference model built from named feature aggregation rather than opaque trained embeddings — consistent with this project's use of recency weighting, play-frequency, and optional influence-track inputs as explicit configuration choices rather than hidden parameters.

Why this matters in practice: profile construction sits between raw data and every later ranking decision. If the listening history is sparse, partially unmatched, or skewed toward recent sessions, the profile inherits those biases and so does every ranking that follows from it. A transparent system needs to surface not only the final preference representation, but the evidence behind it — how many aligned tracks contributed, which weighting rules were applied, whether influence tracks shifted the baseline. Without that visibility, a ranking difference that looks like a scoring effect might actually trace back to the profile, and there is no way to distinguish the two.

Influence tracks fit naturally within this framing. Rather than treating them as an optional add-on, they function as a bounded control surface: the user deliberately adjusts or supplements the profile when their listening history is incomplete or no longer reflects their intent. That is consistent with controllability literature that treats user influence as useful when it is explicit, bounded, and testable [@jin_effects_2020; @andjelkovic_moodplay_2019; @liu_aggregating_2025].

Candidate generation raises the same issues and deserves the same treatment. Playlist systems do not score an entire catalogue — they build a candidate pool first, and what gets into that pool shapes everything downstream. Challenge analyses and evaluation work show that outcomes depend heavily on method composition and how candidates are handled [@zamani_analysis_2019; @teinemaa_composition_2018]. Thresholds that look like efficiency parameters actually determine coverage, diversity, and the range of playlists the system can produce. In an auditable pipeline those thresholds should be logged and available for sensitivity testing, not buried in implementation defaults.

There is also an explanation-quality argument. If a track was excluded before scoring — because of a filter, a threshold, or a missing-feature rule — that fact belongs in the audit trail. Explaining why selected tracks scored well while staying silent about earlier exclusion decisions gives a partial and potentially misleading picture of how the final playlist came to exist. Chapter 3 should make profile aggregation rules, influence-track use, and candidate-threshold logic explicit enough that their effects can be inspected and varied independently.

## 2.5 Music Recommendation and Playlist-Specific Challenges

Music recommendation does not map cleanly onto generic top-$N$ logic. People experience listening sequentially, and they evaluate not just whether individual tracks are good but how they move together — transitions, pacing, repetition, overall shape [@schedl_current_2018]. A set of individually well-ranked tracks can still produce a playlist that feels wrong if the ordering is jarring, artist repetition is too high, or variety breaks internal coherence. Playlist quality is a property of the sequence, not just its constituents [@schedl_current_2018; @bonnin_automated_2015].

Playlist continuation research makes this tension recurring. Coherence, novelty, diversity, and sensible ordering pull against one another and rarely optimise together without deliberate trade-offs [@ferraro_automatic_2018; @vall_feature-combination_2019; @zamani_analysis_2019]. Users perceive playlists as ordered structures, not collections of independent items [@neto_algorithmic_2023]. Evaluation results are also notably protocol-sensitive in this space — reported improvements often depend on method composition and experimental setup more than the papers suggest, so results need to be read alongside the conditions under which they were produced [@teinemaa_composition_2018; @bonnin_automated_2015; @mcfee_million_2012].

Subjective similarity is a separate difficulty, and an underappreciated one. Perception studies show limited inter-rater agreement on music similarity and considerable sensitivity to how the comparison task is framed [@flexer_problem_2016; @siedenburg_modeling_2017]. The semantic gap makes this worse: low-level audio and metadata features do not capture what listeners mean by mood, atmosphere, nostalgia, or tension [@bogdanov_semantic_2013; @deldjoo_content-driven_2024]. Listening context adds further instability — the same person may want a very different playlist for studying, commuting, or a social occasion, often without being able to say exactly why.

None of this makes computational recommendation unworkable, but it does define where interpretation needs to stop. Explicit feature proxies are acceptable; claims that the system has captured the full complexity of listening intent are not. Music4All provides a reasonable corpus for this work given its coverage of metadata, tags, lyrics, and audio-related attributes for content-driven experimentation [@pegoraro_santana_music4all_2020], with supplementary support from third-party multimodal usage where transfer limits remain clearly stated [@ru_improving_2023]. Playlist generation in the design should be treated as a distinct stage with explicit assembly rules, and similarity outputs should be read as auditable approximations rather than objective correctness.

## 2.6 Deterministic Feature-Based Design Rationale with Comparator Context

The core advantage of feature-based recommendation for this project is straightforward: ranking signals stay inspectable. When preference profiles and candidate scores are computed from explicit descriptors, the system can report what drove each decision and roughly by how much [@bogdanov_semantic_2013; @deldjoo_content-driven_2024]. Making execution deterministic adds a second benefit — with fixed inputs and fixed configuration, the same run should produce the same playlist, which is a basic requirement for any reproducibility claim.

Neural and hybrid recommenders can capture richer feature interactions and often achieve stronger predictive performance where training data is plentiful [@cano_hybrid_2017; @he_neural_2017; @liu_multimodal_2025; @yu_self_supervised_2024; @moysis_music_2023]. Choosing a deterministic feature-based design here is not a claim that this approach is generally better. It is a deliberate trade-off: under the constraints of an artefact-scope thesis where inspectability, controllability, observability, and reproducibility are the primary objectives, a theoretically weaker but fully auditable approach is the more defensible one.

Metric sensitivity is worth acknowledging explicitly rather than glossing over. How similarity behaves in feature space depends on specific distance-function choices, and those choices matter [@fkih_similarity_2022; @furini_social_2024]. Broad multi-dataset evidence isolating those effects across different playlist objectives is still limited [@schweiger_impact_2025], which actually reinforces rather than undermines the rationale: metric and weighting choices need to be kept as explicit, logged parameters precisely because their effects are real and should not be quietly absorbed into defaults. Score components, similarity metrics, and feature weights should remain configurable and logged so their effects can be directly tested in Chapter 4.

## 2.7 Cross-Source Alignment Reliability, Reproducibility Governance, and Synthesis

Using cross-source preference data makes entity alignment a core engineering concern, not a preprocessing nicety. User-history records and item-corpus records come from different systems with partially inconsistent identifiers and uneven data quality. Standard entity-resolution practice addresses this by using blocking to narrow candidate pairs before comparison, then applying staged matching with refinement, making uncertainty progressively more visible rather than silently absorbing it [@elmagarmid_duplicate_2007; @allam_improved_2018; @papadakis_blocking_2021; @binette_almost_2022]. The design used here — ISRC-first matching with metadata fallback — follows exactly that staged logic: exact identifiers offer high precision when they are present, and fallback routes recover additional matches when they are absent or inconsistent. Alignment uncertainty is not eliminated, but it becomes part of the audit trail.

Two caveats need to stay explicit. Most of the entity-resolution literature is cross-domain rather than music-specific, and neural matching is a relevant alternative for difficult cases [@barlaug_neural_2021]. But neural approaches add complexity and reduce inspectability unless substantial traceability infrastructure is built around them — a poor trade-off for a system whose primary claim is transparency. For this project, the complexity-auditability balance still favours staged deterministic matching with explicit logging of unmatched cases.

Reproducibility in recommender research is a well-documented problem. Under-specified split definitions, preprocessing steps, and dependency management routinely prevent results from being independently reconstructed [@bellogin_improving_2021; @cavenaghi_systematic_2023; @ferrari_dacrema_troubling_2021; @zhu_bars_2022; @anelli_elliot_2021; @betello_reproducible_2025]. In an artefact thesis, reproducibility is part of what the work needs to demonstrate — it is not something added at the end. Each run should preserve enough state for replay and inspection: input summaries, alignment-path diagnostics, configuration, candidate-pool statistics, score traces, assembly outcomes, and final outputs. Recent music explainability work supports the same underlying principle that explanations should expose how a decision was reached, not just what the outcome was [@sotirou_musiclime_2025].

Pulling this together: the literature supports objective-aligned method selection, mechanism-linked explanation, bounded controllability, explicit profile construction, candidate shaping as a first-class design stage, playlist-level engineering, staged alignment, and run-level governance. What it does not provide is practical end-to-end guidance for integrating all of these concerns into a single pipeline that is simultaneously transparent, controllable, observable, and reproducible under real cross-source data conditions. That specific gap is what this thesis addresses, and the contribution is a design-oriented demonstration of how such a pipeline can be built and evaluated — not a new model class.

Two limitations carry forward into later chapters. Music-specific alignment benchmark evidence is thinner than the broader entity-resolution literature. The source set also lacks wide multi-dataset isolation of deterministic similarity effects across different playlist objectives. These bound how far the findings can be generalised, but they do not undermine the core claim: the contribution is scoped engineering evidence, not a universal ranking of recommender methods. Chapter 3 should operationalise these conclusions into explicit mechanisms for alignment diagnostics, profile construction, candidate filtering, deterministic scoring, playlist assembly, explanation outputs, and run-level logging, while Chapter 4 evaluates them through replayability, fidelity, sensitivity, and rule-compliance evidence.
