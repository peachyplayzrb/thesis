Chapter 2: Literature Review

Foundational Concepts and Scope of Recommender Systems

Music streaming platforms expose users to millions of tracks, creating substantial choice complexity and decision fatigue. Foundational surveys define recommendation as utility estimation under uncertainty, rather than direct preference detection, because available evidence is partial, noisy, and context-dependent (Adomavicius and Tuzhilin, 2005; Lu et al., 2015). While this framework provides a coherent formal basis, it assumes that historical interaction traces reliably proxy user value, even though media consumption behaviour may be shaped by convenience, habit, or interface design rather than stable preferences. This assumption is subsequently challenged across paradigm comparisons, evaluation debates, and music-specific constraint studies.

Interaction logs, such as play counts and session traces, are commonly interpreted as behavioural indicators of preference, but they remain indirect and open to interpretation (Roy and Dutta, 2022). This reliance introduces a structural inconsistency between data scale and validity: although implicit data are plentiful, their causal significance is often ambiguous. Adomavicius and Tuzhilin (2005) treat observed interaction behaviour as a practical utility signal, whereas Roy and Dutta (2022) show that data-source and contextual effects can distort direct preference interpretation. The unresolved disagreement concerns a core assumption: whether interaction frequency primarily reflects preference strength or reflects exposure conditions that only partially represent preference.

A prevalent assumption in recommender research is that higher predictive accuracy corresponds to higher recommendation quality. Methodological analyses challenge this by showing that reported improvements are highly sensitive to preprocessing choices, data-splitting designs, and metric selection (Herlocker et al., 2004; Ferrari Dacrema et al., 2021; Bauer et al., 2024). While benchmark protocols support comparability, they can obscure concerns regarding external validity and explanation fidelity. Accuracy-centered benchmark studies report gains under fixed evaluation conditions, whereas Ferrari Dacrema et al. (2021) and Bauer et al. (2024) question whether those gains remain meaningful outside that setup by showing that when preprocessing and evaluation configurations shift, reproducibility degrades and headline improvements become less stable. This leaves broad accuracy-centered claims methodologically narrow, because many studies evaluate benchmark performance only and often do not test whether users can understand or challenge recommendation logic.

Across these debates, one recurrent tension is clear: assumptions introduced in early modelling choices often carry into later evaluation claims, but many studies assess those stages in isolation rather than as a single evidential chain.

Core Recommendation Paradigms and Associated Trade-offs

Content-based, collaborative, and hybrid systems constitute the primary paradigm families (Adomavicius and Tuzhilin, 2005; Lu et al., 2015), but this taxonomy masks significant disagreement about evidence reliability. Content-based methods prioritise explicit descriptors, collaborative methods emphasise interaction structure, and hybrid methods integrate both. Each paradigm can perform well under certain conditions, yet each rests on different assumptions about trustworthy evidence, producing different failure modes under sparse, noisy, or cross-source data.

Content-based music recommendation relies on metadata, tags, lyrics, and audio descriptors, enabling interpretable feature-level modelling across multiple content layers (Deldjoo et al., 2024; Bogdanov et al., 2013). However, these studies also show that engineered descriptors only partially capture affective and situational aspects of listening. Deldjoo et al. (2024) and Bogdanov et al. (2013) support descriptor-level interpretability, whereas Flexer and Grill (2016) dispute the assumption that feature-space similarity reliably reflects listener-perceived similarity. On comparative strength, Flexer and Grill's inter-rater ceiling argument sets a stricter evidential standard for perceptual claims, because descriptor-level clarity alone does not validate perceptual correspondence.

Collaborative filtering estimates relevance from user-item interaction patterns and often performs well when behavioural matrices are dense (Adomavicius and Tuzhilin, 2005). Zhang and Chen (2020), however, note that latent representations can obscure ranking interpretability. Adomavicius and Tuzhilin (2005) treat dense interaction structure as a strong relevance signal, whereas Ferrari Dacrema et al. (2021) and Bauer et al. (2024) show that protocol and evaluation choices can inflate apparent progress. The evidence is less convincing for cross-context claims: dense-matrix gains are robust under benchmark settings, but support is weaker when the same claims are interpreted as causally informative outside those setups.

Hybrid systems are frequently presented as remedies for weaknesses in single-paradigm approaches (Cano and Morisio, 2017), primarily as a performance remedy that improves aggregate predictive metrics through combined signal sources. However, Zhang and Chen (2020) problematise this at the explanation level by showing that as collaborative and content channels are layered, attribution clarity weakens because rank changes can no longer be traced cleanly to specific input signals. The disagreement is therefore not whether hybrids can work, but what counts as success: higher aggregate performance versus recommendations whose drivers remain interpretable and attributable. For performance-only claims, Cano and Morisio (2017) and He et al. (2017) provide stronger evidence; for explanation-quality claims, Zhang and Chen (2020) provide the stricter standard because they require mechanism-coupled accounts rather than outcome gains alone.

Metric design further intensifies these disagreements. Fkih (2022) treats similarity-function choice as a first-order determinant of recommender behaviour, whereas studies reporting aggregate gains without metric-ablation detail often treat metric choice as secondary. This interpretation is limited by incompatible assumptions: either metric choice is constitutive of observed ranking behaviour, or it is incidental tuning. Overall, the evidence is more consistent with Fkih's sensitivity argument, because studies with limited metric-ablation reporting cannot rule out metric-driven variance in reported gains.

Data sparsity further differentiates paradigm suitability. Collaborative and many hybrid variants generally require dense interaction histories, while feature-oriented systems can operate with sparse or partially unmatched evidence. In sparse cross-source environments, studies report mixed results: additional modelling layers may amplify upstream uncertainty in some cases or improve fit in others. The result is harder to generalise because complexity gains are not consistently separable from data-quality and alignment-quality conditions.

Transparency, Explainability, and User Control in Recommender System Literature

Explanation research has consistently argued that recommendation quality is multidimensional, encompassing transparency, trust, effectiveness, and scrutability alongside predictive utility (Tintarev and Masthoff, 2007, 2012). Later findings, however, show that these goals do not always align: persuasive explanations may increase confidence without improving understanding. Tintarev and Masthoff (2012) also identify methodological risk in how explanation outcomes are operationalised, because different instruments can favor satisfaction-oriented effects over decision-effectiveness effects. Knijnenburg et al. (2012) and Afroogh et al. (2024) sharpen this problem by showing that perceived usefulness and trust vary with user and application context, so instruments centered on satisfaction or confidence can overstate explanation quality when they do not test decision effectiveness or mechanism comprehension. This tension challenges a common assumption in early framework-oriented literature that explanation availability is a sufficient proxy for explanation quality.

Transparency is typically framed as visibility of system logic, explainability as intelligibility of specific outputs, and controllability as user influence over behaviour (Jin et al., 2020). Although these categories are analytically distinct, empirical work indicates they are frequently conflated in evaluation narratives. Zhang and Chen (2020) directly challenge post-hoc plausibility as evidence of explanation fidelity, arguing that persuasive explanations may remain weakly coupled to ranking mechanisms.

Empirical evidence also shows that explanation effects are context-dependent. Jin et al. (2020) report higher recommendation acceptance and perceived usefulness for control-oriented interfaces in some user conditions, whereas Zhang and Chen (2020) challenge quality inferences drawn from perceived usefulness alone. Usability and trust studies (Knijnenburg et al., 2012; Afroogh et al., 2024) further show that user and application context shape outcomes. In evidential terms, mechanism-fidelity criteria set a higher bar than acceptance-only criteria for explanation-quality claims, because acceptance indicators do not test whether users can reconstruct, verify, or challenge the mechanism that produced the ranking.

Because Jin et al. (2020) measure acceptance and perceived usefulness, while Zhang and Chen (2020) question whether such measures demonstrate mechanism fidelity, the literature remains weaker on direct explanation-fidelity validation than on user-perceived usefulness outcomes.

Evidence regarding controllability varies in methodological rigour. Jin et al. (2020) provide the domain-specific music-recommender evidence that concrete interface manipulations can influence user-facing outcomes. Nauta et al. (2023), however, provide the more constraining test for evaluating such claims because they require quantitative assessment rather than anecdotal acceptance alone. On evidential ranking, the stricter standard therefore comes from Nauta et al. (2023), even though Jin et al. (2020) remain the more direct music-domain study. Many studies, however, report control presence without testing whether the observed effect is caused by control actuation itself rather than by concurrent ranking or filtering changes. This leaves a narrower evidential base: interface-level controls are often shown to exist, but fewer studies demonstrate stable, attributable, and repeatable output changes after explicit control-input changes.

Reproducibility studies report similar constraints. Methodological reviews show that under-specified preprocessing, split logic, and implementation details can hinder independent reconstruction (Beel et al., 2016; Anelli et al., 2021; Cavenaghi et al., 2023). Although richer reporting is often proposed as a remedy, reviewed work indicates that reconstruction still fails when key preprocessing and evaluation decisions remain under-specified. This leaves an unresolved gap between outcome-centric reporting practice and reproducibility-oriented reporting expectations.

Preference Evidence, Profile Construction, and Candidate Generation

Profile construction is often described as preprocessing, but literature presents it as a high-impact modelling decision that shapes the evidence entering ranking. Aggregating interaction history can reduce noise, yet it also embeds assumptions about recency, repetition, and signal reliability (Adomavicius and Tuzhilin, 2005; Roy and Dutta, 2022). Roy and Dutta (2022) offer the stronger challenge to neutrality assumptions by showing that behavioural traces can reflect exposure and context effects rather than stable preference.

Content-based music studies support explicit profile representations built from interpretable descriptors (Bogdanov et al., 2013; Deldjoo et al., 2024). Compared to embedding-dominant pipelines, this can make feature contributions easier to interpret. However, omitted descriptors can systematically compress preference space. The resulting conflict is at the assumption level: explicit representation can improve interpretability, but interpretability does not establish that selected features adequately represent listener intent.

Cross-source pipelines intensify these concerns because profile quality depends on alignment completeness and source consistency. If imported history is sparse, temporally skewed, or partially unmatched, downstream ranking inherits these distortions. Literature therefore identifies profile-side uncertainty as analytically prior to score-side interpretation, yet many studies report final ranking confidence with limited profile uncertainty detail.

Explicit user-correction mechanisms, such as influence track injection (Jin et al., 2020) and mood-based interactive filtering (Andjelkovic et al., 2019), are frequently cited as controllability support. Subsequent evidence, however, shows strong dependence on weighting and integration constraints. Reported benefits are therefore conditional, not general: low weighting can make effects negligible, while aggressive weighting can destabilise profile behaviour.

Candidate generation remains under-analysed relative to later ranking stages. Zamani et al. (2019) show strong sensitivity of continuation outcomes to candidate handling, whereas score-centric reporting traditions often treat thresholding primarily as an efficiency choice. This leaves the claim narrow: if candidate filtering determines the feasible ranking set, downstream metric gains cannot be interpreted independently of upstream exclusion logic, especially when candidate-generation effects are not ablated separately from scoring effects.

Zamani et al. (2019), Ferraro et al. (2018), and Vall et al. (2019) jointly indicate that method composition and candidate handling materially affect continuation outcomes, but candidate-pool effects are not always isolated as independent variance sources. The strongest methodological constraint comes from Zamani et al. (2019), because they show that playlist scenario, reranking strategy, and upstream handling choices alter the candidate space before downstream scoring is evaluated; by contrast, Ferraro et al. (2018) and Vall et al. (2019) mainly compare hybrid continuation performance once those upstream conditions are already fixed or conditioned by data availability. This under-specification weakens cross-study comparability of ranking-quality claims because reported gains can reflect both candidate-space construction and ranking behavior when those contributions are not disentangled.

Music Recommendation and Playlist-Specific Challenges

Music recommendation does not align directly with generic top-N retrieval because listener evaluation is sequential and relational rather than purely item-based: acceptance of a track depends partly on preceding tracks and on evolving session context. Schedl et al. (2018) show that short-track consumption, contextual variability, and session dynamics undermine static relevance assumptions. Top-N metrics can capture item-level fit, but they do not directly evaluate whether adjacent transitions are coherent, whether pacing is preserved, or whether temporal flow is maintained across the playlist sequence.

Playlist studies indicate that coherence, diversity, novelty, and order operate as competing objectives rather than jointly maximisable targets (Ferraro et al., 2018; Vall et al., 2019; Bonnin and Jannach, 2015; Schweiger et al., 2025). Some studies present tractable balancing, while others show objective interference where gains in one objective coincide with losses in another. Ferraro et al. (2018) report aggregate continuation gains from hybrid signal integration, whereas Schweiger et al. (2025) show that coherence outcomes are sensitive to distance definitions and playlist characteristics. On evidential ranking, Schweiger et al. (2025) set a higher evidential bar on one-dimensional performance claims, because sensitivity to distance definitions and playlist characteristics means aggregate continuation gains cannot be assumed to reflect sequence-level quality improvement. The evidence is therefore less convincing for one-dimensional performance claims because aggregate gains do not isolate whether sequence-level quality improved or whether only item-level fit shifted.

Perceived music similarity is inherently unstable. Flexer and Grill (2016) report low inter-rater agreement and pronounced framing effects, suggesting that similarity judgments are partly subjective and context-dependent. This literature therefore challenges the assumption that feature-space proximity alone provides behaviourally stable relevance evidence.

Dataset suitability presents an additional constraint. Music4All enables reproducible multimodal experimentation using metadata, tags, lyrics, and audio-related attributes (Pegoraro Santana et al., 2020), and later studies show utility for multimodal genre-related tasks (Ru et al., 2023). However, transfer from representation or classification performance to preference fidelity is not guaranteed, leaving an external-validity gap between dataset affordances and listening intent.

Feature-Based and Latent Approaches: Comparative Strengths and Limitations

Comparative literature on feature-based and latent recommender families identifies a persistent trade-off between inspectability and representational flexibility. Feature-based scoring pipelines support descriptor-level attribution of rank variation (Bogdanov et al., 2013; Deldjoo et al., 2024), but descriptor transparency does not guarantee behavioural adequacy when contextual and affective factors are weakly represented. Latent and hybrid models may improve predictive performance in data-rich settings, yet they can weaken causal inspectability because rank changes are harder to attribute to specific, auditable signal contributions.

Cano and Morisio (2017) and He et al. (2017) report that hybrid and neural models can outperform simpler approaches in data-rich environments, while Zhang and Chen (2020) question the practical sufficiency of such gains by emphasising explanation-fidelity and interpretability deficits in opaque model families. The disagreement concerns evidence quality rather than capacity alone: predictive lift is often demonstrated, but many studies report aggregate gains without isolating which learned components caused those gains.

Sensitivity to similarity metrics remains a central technical caveat for both feature-based and hybrid families. Fkih (2022) treats metric choice as a primary determinant of behaviour, whereas studies lacking metric-ablation detail implicitly downweight that role. Because scaling, normalisation, and descriptor weighting can transform neighbourhood geometry, comparative claims without explicit metric-sensitivity evidence remain methodologically weaker.

Collectively, the literature does not support a universal hierarchy among feature-based, latent, hybrid, and deterministic approaches. A recurring asymmetry remains: optimisation-focused work often under-specifies explanation and evaluation conditions, whereas transparency-focused work often under-specifies representational adequacy under richer preference contexts.

Cross-Source Alignment, Reproducibility, and Synthesis

Cross-source recommendation relies on entity alignment across heterogeneous identifiers and metadata. Entity-resolution literature supports matching pipelines that address combinatorial search and uncertainty, with blocking and filtering explicitly formalised in later surveys (Elmagarmid et al., 2007; Papadakis et al., 2021). Papadakis et al. (2021) also make explicit that blocking and filtering are recall-efficiency trade-offs, so their effectiveness depends on assumptions about token consistency, naming stability, and candidate-generation coverage. While identifier-first matching can achieve high precision, fallback metadata pathways introduce threshold-sensitive ambiguity that ER literature often reports using probabilistic or confidence-scored matching rather than binary outcomes. In music metadata settings, where aliases, remasters, and versioned titles are common, those blocking assumptions are less stable; when low-confidence matches are collapsed into matched/unmatched counts, uncertainty is hidden and downstream ranking confidence can be overstated.

Reproducibility failures in recommender research are frequently attributed to incomplete protocol specification, hidden preprocessing steps, and dependency drift (Ferrari Dacrema et al., 2021; Bellogin and Said, 2021; Zhu et al., 2022; Anelli et al., 2021). Zhu et al. (2022) argue that standardised benchmark infrastructure improves comparability, whereas Bellogin and Said (2021) show that infrastructure alone is insufficient when assumptions and preprocessing choices remain underreported. Bellogin and Said (2021) provide the more constraining test in this comparison: Zhu et al. (2022) standardise datasets, code, hyperparameters, logs, and result formats, but Bellogin and Said show that shared infrastructure does not expose hidden preprocessing logic or force agreement on evaluation procedures, so comparability remains incomplete even when tooling is standardised. This reporting gap is therefore not only procedural but explanatory, because opaque preprocessing and scoring choices also limit later attempts to audit which mechanisms produced an observed output.

Recent music explainability work also indicates that feature-level mechanism explanations can provide clearer accounts of which attributes influence specific outputs than aggregate prediction scores alone (Sotirou et al., 2025). Yet many studies still under-specify how data preparation, candidate handling, and scoring choices interact in reported outcomes.

The literature establishes several robust findings: recommendation quality claims are highly evaluation-dependent, explanation persuasiveness is not equivalent to explanation fidelity, profile construction and candidate generation are first-order modelling decisions, and cross-source matching introduces persistent uncertainty. What remains unresolved is how these constraints can be handled jointly within one evaluative framework. Most studies isolate accuracy, explanation, controllability, or reproducibility, but few evaluate these dimensions together with cross-source reliability.

This gap reflects a broader limitation in current recommender research: evaluation is typically fragmented across separate quality dimensions rather than treated as a system-level problem. Music-specific alignment benchmarks remain underdeveloped, and deterministic similarity effects are rarely compared across multiple datasets and competing playlist objectives. As a result, existing studies provide only partial evidence about recommender quality, making it difficult to judge whether a system is simultaneously transparent, controllable, reproducible, and reliable across sources. The reviewed literature therefore does not yet provide a clear framework for joint evaluation of these dimensions, leaving cross-source recommender quality claims without a shared evaluative basis.
