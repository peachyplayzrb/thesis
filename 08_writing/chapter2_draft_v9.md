# Chapter 2: Literature Review

## 2.1 Foundations, Scope, and Thesis Positioning
Digital platforms require users to choose from very large catalogues, and music streaming makes that problem acute by exposing listeners to millions of tracks with limited time and attention. Recommender systems emerged as a practical response to this information-overload problem by helping users discover items they are likely to value [@adomavicius_toward_2005; @lu_recommender_2015; @roy_systematic_2022]. Foundational recommender research frames this task as utility estimation under partial information: infer likely value from sparse and incomplete evidence rather than retrieve a fully known answer [@adomavicius_toward_2005; @roy_systematic_2022]. That framing matters here because it makes clear that recommendation behaviour is shaped by the type of evidence collected, the representation chosen for users and items, and the objective used to judge success.

It also clarifies the status of listening history in this thesis. Preference evidence in recommender systems may be explicit, such as ratings or likes, or implicit, such as clicks, playback history, or other behavioural traces [@adomavicius_toward_2005; @roy_systematic_2022]. The project therefore works with listening history as one form of implicit preference evidence. That evidence is useful, but it still needs explicit engineering choices for aggregation, filtering, and explanation.

The goal of this thesis is not to propose a new recommender model family or maximize benchmark performance. It is to engineer an automated playlist-generation pipeline that remains transparent, controllable, observable, and reproducible within BSc-level constraints. Evaluation literature supports this objective-first stance. Recommender outcomes vary with protocol definitions, preprocessing decisions, and metric framing, so strong benchmark performance under one setup does not automatically make a method the best choice for a project whose main contribution is inspectable engineering [@herlocker_evaluating_2004; @ferrari_dacrema_troubling_2021; @anelli_elliot_2021; @bauer_exploring_2024]. Method choice should therefore follow contribution goals rather than benchmark scores in isolation [@jannach_measuring_2019].

This chapter is structured as a narrowing argument. It moves from general recommender trade-offs to transparency and evaluation concerns, then to preference evidence, music-specific constraints, deterministic design, and cross-source governance. The aim is not to collect citations but to reduce design uncertainty for the architecture in Chapter 3.

Design consequence: Chapter 3 should justify architecture choices against transparency, controllability, observability, and reproducibility objectives rather than accuracy claims alone.

## 2.2 Core Recommendation Paradigms and Their Trade-offs
Recommender systems are commonly grouped into content-based, collaborative, and hybrid families [@adomavicius_toward_2005; @lu_recommender_2015; @cano_hybrid_2017]. The taxonomy is useful for orientation, but the literature does not support treating one family as universally best. Suitability depends on what evidence is available, how much complexity is acceptable, and what kind of explanation or control the system must support.

Content-based methods infer preference from item attributes. In music settings, these attributes can include metadata, tags, lyrics, and audio descriptors. Their main relevance here is mechanism visibility: when ranking depends on explicit features, the rationale for recommendation can often be traced back to those same features [@deldjoo_content-driven_2024; @bogdanov_semantic_2013]. Collaborative methods instead infer relevance from interaction patterns across users and items. They can perform strongly at scale with rich histories, but direct interpretability may weaken when ranking logic is encoded in latent relationships rather than explicit feature comparisons [@adomavicius_toward_2005; @zhang_explainable_2020]. Hybrid systems combine signals to exploit complementary strengths, yet the combination itself introduces additional reasoning complexity that can make explanation and audit harder unless interpretability is designed in from the start [@cano_hybrid_2017].

Similarity modelling makes these trade-offs concrete. Metric definitions, normalization choices, and threshold settings all shape neighbourhood construction and ranking behaviour [@fkih_similarity_2022]. Those are mechanism-level decisions, not implementation trivia. If they remain explicit, behaviour can be inspected and challenged; if they remain buried in defaults, claims about explanation and control weaken.

Data conditions reinforce the same point. Collaborative and hybrid methods often benefit from denser interaction data, while transparent feature-based methods can remain workable when interaction logs are sparse, incomplete, or uneven across entities. In a cross-source pipeline, such unevenness is normal. Under those conditions, a theoretically stronger model in ideal settings may still be less defensible than a simpler approach whose inputs, failures, and outputs can be audited.

Design consequence: feature selection, similarity metric choice, normalization, and weighting should be treated as explicit and reviewable design commitments.

## 2.3 Transparency, Explainability, Controllability, Observability, and Evaluation
Explainable recommender research argues that predictive quality alone is insufficient when users or evaluators need to understand and question system behaviour [@tintarev_survey_2007; @tintarev_evaluating_2012; @zhang_explainable_2020]. For this thesis, four terms need to remain distinct. Transparency means visibility of recommendation logic. Explainability means understandable reasons for specific outputs. Controllability means user influence through explicit inputs or parameters [@tintarev_survey_2007; @balog_transparent_2019; @jin_effects_2020]. Observability, in this thesis, means run-level visibility of execution state, intermediate diagnostics, and output traces.

These distinctions matter because explanation goals can conflict. Explanations may increase trust or satisfaction without improving genuine understanding [@tintarev_evaluating_2012; @nauta_anecdotal_2023]. Post-hoc explanations can be persuasive yet weakly coupled to the mechanism that produced the ranking [@zhang_explainable_2020]. For a transparency-oriented artefact, explanations should therefore remain as close as possible to the actual scoring and rule-application process. Interface research adds an additional constraint: faithful explanations must still be understandable to non-experts [@tsai_explaining_2018; @knijnenburg_explaining_2012; @afroogh_trust_2024].

The literature also implies that evaluation should be purpose-specific. If the thesis claims controllability, it is not enough to expose a control in the interface; changing that control should create interpretable downstream effects [@jin_effects_2020; @nauta_anecdotal_2023]. If it claims transparency, explanation values should remain faithful to underlying score components. If it claims observability and reproducibility, runs should be documented through recorded configuration and stage-level diagnostics [@beel_towards_2016; @bellogin_improving_2021; @cavenaghi_systematic_2023; @anelli_elliot_2021].

This leads to a process-plus-outcome evaluation frame. Process evidence checks whether the pipeline behaved as designed at each stage: whether alignment paths were recorded, whether candidate thresholds changed pool size as expected, whether score traces can be reconstructed, and whether playlist rules were actually enforced. Outcome evidence checks whether outputs changed in interpretable ways after parameter or input changes: for example, whether added influence tracks shift the ranked list, whether changing a feature weight alters score composition in the expected direction, and whether replaying the same run reproduces the same playlist. That framing fits the thesis scope because it does not require large-scale user studies to produce defensible evidence, but it does require disciplined reporting of explanations, parameter sensitivity, rule compliance, and replayability.

Design consequence: Chapter 3 should generate mechanism-linked explanations, explicit controls, and run-level diagnostics so Chapter 4 can test explanation fidelity, parameter sensitivity, and replayability rather than rely on narrative claims.

## 2.4 Preference Evidence, Profile Construction, and Candidate Shaping
The project depends on imported listening history, so the literature review needs to treat profile construction as a first-class design issue rather than an implementation detail. Listening history provides implicit preference evidence rather than a fully specified user statement [@adomavicius_toward_2005; @roy_systematic_2022]. Because of that ambiguity, building a user profile always involves interpretation. In this thesis, the defensible choice is not to treat implicit feedback as objective ground truth, but to aggregate it through explicit, reviewable rules.

Content-based music work supports this direction. Bogdanov et al. show that user preference representations can be built from explicit preference examples using semantic audio descriptors [@bogdanov_semantic_2013]. Deldjoo et al. frame content-driven systems around explicit content layers and note ongoing challenges in transparency, sequence recommendation, and efficiency [@deldjoo_content-driven_2024]. Together, these sources support a preference model built from inspectable feature aggregation rather than opaque latent training. That is consistent with the project's use of recency, play-frequency, and optional influence-track weighting as explicit configuration choices rather than hidden model parameters.

This point is methodologically important because profile construction sits between raw data and every later ranking decision. If imported history is sparse, partially unmatched, or skewed toward recent sessions, the profile inherits those biases. A transparent system should therefore expose not only the final preference representation but also the coverage of evidence used to build it, including how many aligned tracks contributed, which weighting rules were applied, and where influence tracks altered the baseline profile. Without that visibility, later ranking differences can be mistaken for scoring effects when they actually originate in profile construction.

Manual influence tracks are also defensible within this framing. They should not be treated as an ad hoc extra input, but as a bounded control surface that lets the user deliberately bias or refine the profile when historical listening evidence is incomplete or noisy. This matches the thesis assumption that influence tracks can complement imported history, and it aligns with controllability literature that treats user influence as useful when it is explicit, bounded, and testable rather than unconstrained [@jin_effects_2020; @andjelkovic_moodplay_2019; @liu_aggregating_2025].

Candidate generation deserves the same status. Playlist systems do not score the entire catalogue in a conceptual vacuum; they first shape a candidate pool. Challenge analyses and implementation reports show that outcomes depend on method composition, candidate handling, and evaluation protocol [@zamani_analysis_2019; @teinemaa_composition_2018]. Thresholds therefore influence not just efficiency but also coverage, diversity, and the space of possible final playlists. In a deterministic and auditable pipeline, candidate thresholds and pruning rules should be explicit, logged, and available for sensitivity testing.

Treating candidate generation as a separate stage also improves explanation quality. If a track never entered the candidate pool because a threshold, filter, or missing-feature rule excluded it, that fact belongs to the audit trail. Otherwise, the system can explain why selected tracks scored highly while remaining silent about earlier exclusion decisions that shaped the final outcome just as strongly.

Design consequence: Chapter 3 should make profile aggregation rules, influence-track use, and candidate-threshold logic explicit enough that their downstream effects can be inspected and varied one factor at a time.

## 2.5 Music Recommendation and Playlist-Specific Challenges
Music recommendation does not transfer cleanly from generic top-$N$ logic. Listening is often session-based and sequential, and users judge not only individual tracks but transitions, pacing, repetition, and overall flow [@schedl_current_2018]. Playlist quality therefore depends on sequence construction, not only item relevance [@schedl_current_2018; @bonnin_automated_2015]. A playlist of individually strong tracks can still fail if transitions are abrupt, artist repetition is high, or diversity disrupts coherence [@ferraro_automatic_2018; @vall_feature-combination_2019].

Playlist continuation studies repeatedly show tensions among coherence, novelty, diversity, and ordering [@ferraro_automatic_2018; @vall_feature-combination_2019; @zamani_analysis_2019]. Sequencing analyses reinforce that users perceive playlists as ordered structures, not isolated items [@neto_algorithmic_2023]. Benchmark analyses also caution against decontextualized claims. Reported improvements depend on protocol choices and method composition [@teinemaa_composition_2018; @bonnin_automated_2015; @mcfee_million_2012]. Results should therefore be interpreted together with their evaluation setup.

A second challenge is subjective similarity. Music-similarity studies show limited inter-rater agreement and sensitivity to framing, which means computational similarity is better treated as a useful approximation than as objective truth [@flexer_problem_2016; @siedenburg_modeling_2017]. The semantic gap sharpens that limitation: low-level descriptors do not fully capture human concepts such as mood, nostalgia, or atmosphere [@bogdanov_semantic_2013; @deldjoo_content-driven_2024]. Context further complicates matters because the same listener may prefer different playlists for focus, commuting, exercise, or social settings.

These constraints do not invalidate computational recommendation, but they do define interpretation boundaries. In this thesis, context claims should remain bounded by what the system can actually observe and represent. Explicit feature proxies are acceptable; claims that the system captures the full complexity of listening intent are not. Music4All is suitable here because it offers metadata, tags, lyrics, and audio-related attributes for content-driven experimentation [@pegoraro_santana_music4all_2020]. Third-party usage in multimodal studies provides additional support while transfer limits remain explicit [@ru_improving_2023].

Design consequence: playlist generation should remain a distinct stage with explicit assembly rules, and similarity outputs should be interpreted as auditable evidence rather than claims to objective musical correctness.

## 2.6 Deterministic Feature-Based Design Rationale with Comparator Context
Given the thesis objectives, feature-based recommendation has a practical advantage: ranking signals remain inspectable. When preference profiles and candidate scores are built from explicit descriptors, the system can report what contributed to each decision and by how much [@bogdanov_semantic_2013; @deldjoo_content-driven_2024]. Deterministic execution adds a second benefit: with fixed input and fixed configuration, outputs should be replayable.

Comparator context remains important. Hybrid and neural recommenders can capture richer interactions and often deliver strong predictive performance [@cano_hybrid_2017; @he_neural_2017; @liu_multimodal_2025; @yu_self_supervised_2024; @moysis_music_2023]. Choosing a deterministic feature-based design here is not a claim of general superiority. It is a constrained trade-off in favour of inspectability, controllability, observability, and reproducibility under a bounded artefact scope.

Metric sensitivity remains a key caveat. Similarity behaviour depends on explicit distance-function choices in feature space [@fkih_similarity_2022; @furini_social_2024]. Music-domain studies strengthen the case for treating those choices as consequential, while broad multi-dataset isolation evidence across playlist objectives remains limited [@schweiger_impact_2025]. That does not undermine the design rationale; it strengthens the need to surface metric and weighting choices as first-order parameters rather than accidental defaults.

Design consequence: score components, similarity metrics, and feature weights should remain configurable and logged so their effects can be tested directly in Chapter 4.

## 2.7 Cross-Source Alignment Reliability, Reproducibility Governance, and Synthesis
Because this project uses cross-source preference data, alignment is a core engineering issue. User-history records and candidate-corpus records come from different systems with partially inconsistent identifiers and uneven data quality. Entity-resolution research usually handles this with blocking and filtering to narrow the candidate set, followed by staged matching and refinement [@elmagarmid_duplicate_2007; @allam_improved_2018; @papadakis_blocking_2021; @binette_almost_2022]. Within this thesis scope, ISRC-first matching with metadata fallback fits that staged logic: exact identifiers offer high precision when available, while fallback routes recover additional links when identifiers are absent or inconsistent. Uncertainty is not removed, but it becomes visible.

Evidence strength still needs to remain bounded. Much of the alignment support is cross-domain rather than music-benchmark specific, and neural entity matching remains a relevant comparator for difficult cases [@barlaug_neural_2021]. However, neural matching introduces additional complexity and inspectability costs unless substantial traceability layers are added. For this MVP, the complexity-auditability trade-off still favours deterministic staged matching with explicit unmatched-case logging.

The same governance logic applies to reproducibility. Recommender-system literature repeatedly reports weak reproducibility when split definitions, preprocessing, dependencies, and configuration states are under-specified [@bellogin_improving_2021; @cavenaghi_systematic_2023; @ferrari_dacrema_troubling_2021; @zhu_bars_2022; @anelli_elliot_2021; @betello_reproducible_2025]. In an artefact thesis, reproducibility is part of the contribution, not post-hoc polish. Operationally, each run should preserve enough information for replay and audit: input summaries, alignment-path counts, configuration, candidate-pool diagnostics, score traces, assembly outcomes, and final outputs. Recent music explainability work supports the same broader principle that explanations should expose contribution structure rather than only final outcomes [@sotirou_musiclime_2025].

The synthesis is now more complete than in earlier drafts. The literature supports objective-aligned method selection, mechanism-linked explanation, bounded controllability, explicit profile construction, candidate shaping as a first-class stage, playlist-level engineering, staged alignment, and run-level governance. What remains comparatively underdeveloped in the source set is practical end-to-end guidance that integrates all of these concerns into one transparent, controllable, and observable pipeline. That is the specific gap this thesis addresses. The contribution is not a new model class, but a design-oriented demonstration of how such a pipeline can be engineered and evaluated under cross-source data conditions.

Residual limitations remain explicit. Music-specific alignment benchmark evidence is still weaker than broader entity-resolution evidence, and the current source set still does not provide broad multi-dataset isolation of deterministic similarity-function effects across multiple playlist objectives. Those limits bound generalization, but they do not undermine the core thesis claim because the contribution is scoped engineering evidence rather than a universal ranking of recommender methods.

Design consequence: Chapter 3 should operationalise these conclusions into explicit mechanisms for alignment diagnostics, profile construction, candidate filtering, deterministic scoring, playlist assembly, explanation outputs, and run-level logging, while Chapter 4 evaluates them through replayability, fidelity, sensitivity, and rule-compliance evidence.
