# Chapter 2: Literature Review (V4)

## 2.1 Recommender Systems Foundations and the Information Overload Problem
The rapid growth of digital catalogues has made content discovery a persistent design problem across online platforms. In practice, users are expected to navigate large item spaces where manual search and comparison quickly become inefficient. Recommender systems emerged as a response to this information-overload condition by estimating what items are likely to be useful for a given user and reducing search effort [@adomavicius_toward_2005; @lu_recommender_2015; @roy_systematic_2022].

Foundational recommender formulations describe recommendation as utility estimation over users and items, typically with sparse preference signals gathered from explicit or implicit feedback [@adomavicius_toward_2005; @roy_systematic_2022]. This framing remains useful because it explains why recommendation is not only a retrieval problem, but also an inference problem under partial information. However, the same framing also highlights an important limitation for thesis design work: utility estimation choices are never neutral. They depend on data availability, feature representation, and objective priorities.

For that reason, contemporary survey work increasingly treats recommender design as context-sensitive rather than algorithm-agnostic [@roy_systematic_2022; @jannach_measuring_2019]. A model family that performs well in one benchmark setup can become a weaker choice in another setting with different constraints, goals, or accountability requirements. This matters directly for the present thesis, which is artefact-based and scoped around transparent, controllable, and observable pipeline behavior rather than leaderboard optimization.

The chapter therefore treats literature as design evidence. The aim is not to identify a universally best recommender method, but to establish which design considerations are defensible for engineering a deterministic playlist-generation pipeline using cross-source music preference data. This framing sets up the transition from general recommender concepts to method trade-offs and explainability concerns.

## 2.2 Core Recommendation Paradigms and Similarity Modelling Trade-offs
Most recommender systems are still discussed through three core paradigms: content-based, collaborative, and hybrid approaches [@adomavicius_toward_2005; @lu_recommender_2015; @cano_hybrid_2017]. Content-based approaches infer preference from item attributes and are useful when interpretable item descriptors are available. Collaborative approaches rely on interaction patterns among users and items, often achieving strong predictive results when interaction data is rich. Hybrid approaches combine signals to offset weaknesses in any single paradigm.

Although this taxonomy is standard, literature across evaluation and engineering practice shows that paradigm labels alone do not determine suitability [@herlocker_evaluating_2004; @ferrari_dacrema_troubling_2021; @anelli_elliot_2021]. Performance and practical utility are shaped by protocol, preprocessing, metric choice, and objective framing. This is why high-level method comparisons should be interpreted as trade-off maps rather than ranking tables.

Similarity modelling is a key example of this trade-off logic. Whether similarity is computed with cosine, Euclidean, correlation-based, or related measures changes how closeness is operationalized in recommendation behavior [@fkih_similarity_2022]. In content-oriented systems, these choices are especially visible because similarity functions directly influence candidate ranking and can often be inspected. In latent or highly composite systems, the same effect can be harder to trace.

For this thesis, similarity modelling is not just a technical detail. It is a mechanism-level design decision connected to transparency and controllability. If similarity definitions, normalization steps, and weights are explicit, recommendation behavior can be traced and challenged. If they are hidden or weakly documented, explanation and control claims become difficult to defend. This motivates the next section, which examines how modern recommender complexity has intensified opacity concerns.

## 2.3 Increasing Model Complexity and Opacity in Modern Recommenders
Recommender research has steadily expanded from classical memory-based formulations toward increasingly complex model stacks, including hybrid pipelines and neural architectures [@cano_hybrid_2017; @he_neural_2017; @liu_multimodal_2025; @yu_self_supervised_2024]. These approaches often improve predictive capability by capturing richer nonlinear interactions and multimodal signals. Their strengths are real and should not be discounted.

At the same time, increased representational power often comes with increased opacity. As models become deeper and more composite, it becomes harder to explain why a specific item was recommended, which component dominated a decision, or how a parameter change propagated through the pipeline [@zhang_explainable_2020; @ferrari_dacrema_troubling_2021]. From an engineering perspective, this raises reproducibility and audit concerns in addition to user-facing interpretability concerns.

Evaluation literature reinforces this point by showing that apparent gains are sensitive to benchmark protocol, preprocessing, and reporting discipline [@herlocker_evaluating_2004; @jannach_measuring_2019; @bauer_exploring_2024]. A strong score under one setup does not automatically transfer into robust practical evidence, especially when decision logic is difficult to inspect. This does not invalidate high-capacity models, but it does mean that complexity introduces additional governance demands.

Within this thesis scope, complexity is therefore treated as a trade-off variable rather than an automatic objective. The design target is a pipeline that can be inspected, controlled, and replayed with traceable artifacts. That target prioritizes explicit mechanism visibility over maximum representational complexity, and it motivates a closer look at transparency, explainability, user control, and observability as explicit research concerns.

## 2.4 Transparency, Explainability, User Control, and Observability as Design Concerns
Explainable recommender research consistently argues that accuracy alone is an incomplete success criterion when systems are expected to support user understanding, trust calibration, or scrutiny [@tintarev_survey_2007; @tintarev_evaluating_2012; @zhang_explainable_2020]. A recommender can perform well on ranking metrics yet still fail as a usable or accountable system if users cannot understand or influence its behavior.

To keep concepts precise, this chapter uses the following working distinctions. Transparency refers to visibility of recommendation logic. Explainability refers to human-understandable reasons for specific outputs. Interpretability refers to the degree to which internal behavior can be understood at model or mechanism level. Controllability refers to user influence through explicit inputs or parameters. Observability refers to run-level visibility for audit and diagnosis, including configuration state, intermediate diagnostics, and output traces [@tintarev_survey_2007; @tintarev_evaluating_2012; @balog_transparent_2019; @jin_effects_2020].

These distinctions matter because explanation goals are not always aligned. Explanations can support trust, persuasion, scrutability, or decision effectiveness, but improvement in one goal may not imply improvement in another [@tintarev_evaluating_2012; @nauta_anecdotal_2023]. As a result, explanation should be engineered and evaluated against explicit purpose.

The literature also differentiates post-hoc explanations from transparency-by-design systems [@zhang_explainable_2020]. Post-hoc explanations may offer plausible narratives after ranking, but they are not always faithful to the true decision path. Transparency-by-design approaches instead keep explanation artifacts linked to the same mechanisms used for scoring and rule application. For this thesis, that linkage is central: explanation quality is strongest when recommendation outputs can be reconstructed from recorded contributors and constraints.

User-control research in music recommendation supports the same engineering discipline. Explicit controls can improve interaction quality, but effects vary by user characteristics and context [@jin_effects_2020; @andjelkovic_moodplay_2019]. Therefore, controllability is treated here as a measurable property, not a rhetorical claim. A control is meaningful only if changing it leads to interpretable downstream effects.

Observability is the condition that makes such effects testable. If control settings change but intermediate behavior is not logged, controllability remains nominal. For an artefact-based thesis, observability is therefore not optional tooling; it is part of the contribution quality because it supports replay, audit, and critical interpretation.

This transparency-control-observability frame transitions naturally into music-specific literature, where sequence structure, subjective similarity, and context effects make these design concerns even more consequential.

## 2.5 Music Recommendation and Playlist Generation Challenges
Music recommendation introduces domain constraints that are less pronounced in many other recommendation settings [@schedl_current_2018; @deldjoo_content-driven_2024]. Consumption is often sequential and session-based, and user judgments depend on flow, transition quality, repetition tolerance, and contextual suitability. Consequently, playlist quality cannot be reduced to independent item relevance.

Playlist-generation studies repeatedly identify tensions among coherence, diversity, novelty, and ordering [@ferraro_automatic_2018; @vall_feature-combination_2019; @neto_algorithmic_2023]. Improving one dimension can weaken another, and outcomes depend on how candidate retrieval, scoring, and assembly constraints are composed. Challenge-level analyses and implementation reports reinforce that protocol and component composition materially shape reported results [@zamani_analysis_2019; @teinemaa_composition_2018; @bonnin_automated_2015]. Team-level challenge reports are treated here as complementary implementation evidence rather than as standalone theoretical foundations.

The music domain also sharpens similarity limitations. Research on music similarity reports limited inter-rater agreement and strong dependence on framing and feature emphasis [@flexer_problem_2016; @siedenburg_modeling_2017]. This indicates that similarity metrics are useful approximations rather than objective truth conditions. It also supports careful claim discipline in later chapters: deterministic similarity can support traceable decision making without claiming universal musical correctness.

A related issue is the semantic gap between low-level computable features and higher-level human music concepts [@bogdanov_semantic_2013; @deldjoo_content-driven_2024]. In practice, systems rely on proxy descriptors and must report limitations explicitly. Context adds further variability, because listening preferences shift with activity, mood, and setting [@schedl_current_2018]. For a scoped artefact, this supports bounded context claims rather than broad generalization.

Dataset choice is part of the same design logic. Music4All is defensible as a canonical corpus because it offers multi-signal resources suitable for reproducible content-driven experimentation [@pegoraro_santana_music4all_2020], with independent usage in related benchmark-informed work [@ru_improving_2023]. The claim remains scoped to thesis suitability, not universal dataset optimality.

These domain findings motivate the next step of the narrative funnel: explicit feature-based representations that support transparent scoring and controllable playlist generation.

## 2.6 Feature-Based Representation and Deterministic Pipeline Rationale
Feature-based representations are central to this thesis because they keep recommendation signals inspectable. In content-driven music settings, tracks can be represented through structured descriptors derived from metadata and audio features [@bogdanov_semantic_2013; @deldjoo_content-driven_2024]. This enables recommendation behavior to be analyzed as explicit relationships in feature space rather than opaque latent interactions.

A practical advantage of this approach is explanatory traceability. When ranking is built on explicit feature comparisons, the system can report which attributes influenced a recommendation and by how much. This is where commonly used Spotify-style attributes such as tempo, energy, danceability, acousticness, valence, loudness, instrumentalness, and speechiness become useful engineering proxies: they provide interpretable dimensions for profile construction, similarity scoring, and user control [@bogdanov_semantic_2013; @deldjoo_content-driven_2024].

Deterministic execution extends that traceability. Under fixed inputs and fixed configuration, a deterministic pipeline should produce stable outputs, making replay and sensitivity analysis feasible [@beel_towards_2016; @bellogin_improving_2021]. This does not imply deterministic systems are always more accurate than high-capacity alternatives. It implies they are often easier to audit, explain, and control under constrained engineering goals.

Comparator context remains important. Hybrid and neural recommender families can offer stronger predictive behavior in many settings [@cano_hybrid_2017; @he_neural_2017; @liu_multimodal_2025; @yu_self_supervised_2024]. The present thesis does not reject those approaches. It adopts a different design objective: prioritize interpretability, traceability, and user-facing control in an artefact whose claims must be defended through clear mechanism-level evidence.

Metric sensitivity is a bounded caveat inside this rationale. Similarity and distance definitions can materially change ranking outcomes [@fkih_similarity_2022; @furini_social_2024; @schweiger_impact_2025]. Therefore, metric choice, normalization, and weighting should be treated as explicit design decisions, not hidden defaults. Current evidence supports this requirement while leaving a known limitation: broad multi-dataset isolation of deterministic similarity-function effects across multiple playlist objectives remains limited in the present source set.

With that rationale established, the chapter can state the literature gap and thesis motivation in a focused way.

## 2.7 Research Gap and Chapter Conclusion
The reviewed literature strongly covers individual ingredients relevant to this thesis: recommender paradigms, explainability concerns, music-specific playlist challenges, feature-based modelling, and reproducibility governance. What remains comparatively underdeveloped in the current evidence set is practical end-to-end guidance for engineering one coherent pipeline that integrates these concerns under explicit transparency, controllability, and observability objectives.

That is the gap addressed by this thesis. The contribution is not a new machine-learning model family. It is an artefact-based engineering demonstration showing how a deterministic, feature-based playlist-generation pipeline can be built and evaluated so that behavior remains inspectable, controllable, observable, and reproducible when operating on cross-source music preference data.

The synthesis supporting this contribution is consistent across sections. Method choice should be objective-aligned rather than benchmark-driven in isolation [@roy_systematic_2022; @jannach_measuring_2019], and explanation should remain mechanism-linked rather than purely post-hoc [@zhang_explainable_2020; @tintarev_evaluating_2012]. Controllability should be demonstrated through interpretable parameter effects [@jin_effects_2020; @nauta_anecdotal_2023], while playlist constraints should be engineered explicitly rather than assumed to emerge from scoring alone [@schedl_current_2018; @vall_feature-combination_2019]. Alignment should be staged and uncertainty-aware [@allam_improved_2018; @papadakis_blocking_2021], and reproducibility plus observability should be treated as evidence-quality requirements rather than optional implementation details [@beel_towards_2016; @anelli_elliot_2021; @betello_reproducible_2025].

Residual limitations remain explicit. Music-specific alignment benchmark evidence is less mature than domain-general entity-resolution evidence, and broad isolation studies on deterministic similarity-function effects across multiple playlist objectives remain limited. These are bounded evidence-depth constraints that qualify interpretation, not blockers to the scoped thesis contribution.

Overall, this chapter provides a coherent literature basis for the research question and the artefact contribution framing. It moves from foundational recommender concepts to domain-specific design motivations and establishes why a transparent, controllable, and observable deterministic playlist-generation pipeline is a defensible engineering target under thesis scope.
