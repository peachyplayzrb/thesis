# Chapter 2: Literature Review (V2)

## 2.1 Foundations, Scope Positioning, and Artefact Framing
Recommender systems are a standard response to information overload in large item collections, but the literature does not support a single universally best architectural family for every setting [@adomavicius_toward_2005; @lu_recommender_2015; @roy_systematic_2022]. Content-based, collaborative, and hybrid approaches remain the dominant high-level categories [@adomavicius_toward_2005; @cano_hybrid_2017]. That taxonomy is useful for positioning alternatives, yet method-family labels alone are insufficient for design choice. Reported value depends on data conditions, operational goals, metric selection, preprocessing decisions, and protocol design [@herlocker_evaluating_2004; @jannach_measuring_2019; @ferrari_dacrema_troubling_2021].

This point matters directly for an artefact-based thesis. This thesis is positioned not as a proposal for a new predictive model family, but as an engineering demonstration of how a transparent, controllable, and observable automated playlist generation pipeline can be built and evaluated using deterministic feature-based recommendation methods. The literature review must therefore do more than summarize recommender-system categories. It must justify which design choices are appropriate for a constrained artefact whose success criteria emphasize inspectability, controllability, replayability, and traceable evaluation rather than leaderboard optimization.

Content-based approaches are especially relevant when preference inference must be tied to explicit item attributes and when user-to-user linkage is unavailable or out of scope [@lu_recommender_2015; @deldjoo_content-driven_2024]. Collaborative and hybrid approaches often produce strong predictive results at scale, but they also tend to depend on interaction structures, latent factors, or multi-component pipelines that can weaken direct mechanism-level transparency unless extra interpretability layers are added [@adomavicius_toward_2005; @zhang_explainable_2020; @cano_hybrid_2017]. Hybrid and neural methods are therefore comparator context, not strawmen. Their presence in the literature sharpens the thesis question: if the artefact prioritizes transparency, controllability, and observability under BSc-feasible constraints, which engineering choices are most defensible?

Evaluation literature reinforces this framing. Recommender-system results are highly sensitive to experimental setup, metric choice, negative-sampling assumptions, and preprocessing choices [@herlocker_evaluating_2004; @ferrari_dacrema_troubling_2021; @anelli_elliot_2021; @bauer_exploring_2024]. A method that wins under one benchmark protocol is not automatically the strongest fit for an artefact whose purpose is to expose its internal decision logic and support exact replay under fixed configuration. This thesis therefore adopts a goal-aligned selection logic: the preferred design is the one that best supports the locked artefact qualities, not the one with the strongest abstract performance narrative.

In that sense, the research question is explicitly engineering-oriented. It asks what design considerations shape the construction of a transparent, controllable, and observable playlist-generation pipeline using cross-source music preference data. The literature is therefore used here as decision support for artefact construction. Evidence is interpreted according to whether it strengthens or weakens choices about scoring transparency, user control, alignment reliability, sequence-aware playlist assembly, and run-level observability.

Design consequence: Chapter 3 should justify architecture choices against transparency, controllability, observability, and reproducibility goals rather than against accuracy claims in isolation.

## 2.2 Transparency, Explainability, Controllability, and Observability
Explainable recommender research consistently argues that predictive accuracy is not a sufficient success criterion when people must understand, trust, inspect, or challenge recommendations [@tintarev_survey_2007; @tintarev_evaluating_2012; @zhang_explainable_2020]. This matters especially in an artefact-based thesis because the value of the artefact is not only whether it outputs plausible tracks, but whether its behavior can be examined and justified. A pipeline that produces recommendations without exposing why they appear would be misaligned with the stated contribution, even if its outputs looked superficially reasonable.

The literature also shows that explanation quality is multidimensional. Explanations can aim to persuade, justify, educate, support trust, enable scrutability, or improve decision quality, and these aims can conflict [@tintarev_evaluating_2012]. An explanation that increases user satisfaction may not improve actual understanding. This means explanation cannot be treated as a decorative add-on. The intended purpose of the explanation must be declared, and the explanation mechanism must be evaluated against that purpose.

The distinction between post-hoc explanation and transparency-by-design is therefore central [@zhang_explainable_2020]. Post-hoc explanations may communicate a plausible reason after a ranking decision, but they are not always tightly coupled to the true decision mechanism. For an artefact that claims transparency and observability, this is a serious design issue. Stronger auditability comes from mechanism-linked explanations that expose the same features, weights, and rule adjustments that actually influenced the recommendation outcome. In practical terms, that means explanation output should be reconstructable from the scoring pipeline itself rather than generated as a loosely related narrative layer.

User-interface and scrutability research adds another constraint. A faithful explanation is not automatically a useful one [@tsai_explaining_2018; @balog_transparent_2019; @knijnenburg_explaining_2012; @afroogh_trust_2024]. If explanation outputs are too technical, users may be unable to interpret them. The engineering challenge is therefore dual: preserve fidelity to the underlying mechanism while also producing an explanation format that remains human-readable. For this thesis, that supports a design in which explanation artifacts expose structured score contributors and rule traces that can be rendered clearly, rather than opaque model internals or generic natural-language summaries.

Controllability introduces a related but distinct requirement. Studies in music recommendation suggest that explicit user influence can be beneficial, but the effects are user-dependent and context-sensitive [@jin_effects_2020; @andjelkovic_moodplay_2019; @liu_aggregating_2025]. This does not justify a claim that any given control scheme will help all users. It does justify treating user controls as a legitimate design concern. Within the locked scope of this thesis, controllability is not framed as unrestricted personalization, but as measurable influence over system behavior through explicit parameters or input signals.

For an artefact-based contribution, controllability must be operational rather than rhetorical. A parameter qualifies as a meaningful control only if changing it leads to interpretable downstream effects in candidate selection, scoring, or playlist assembly. Observability is what makes those effects auditable. If control inputs are changed but the pipeline does not record intermediate behavior, then the system may be configurable without being genuinely inspectable. In this thesis, observability therefore means that execution context, parameter settings, intermediate diagnostics, and output traces are recorded in a way that supports audit and replay.

Recommender-evaluation literature supports this combined framing. Explanation claims should be supported by mechanism-linked evidence rather than anecdotal description, and evaluation should report the relationship between configured inputs, intermediate states, and produced outputs [@tintarev_evaluating_2012; @nauta_anecdotal_2023]. For a BSc artefact, this does not require a large-scale user study. It does require disciplined logging of configuration, score composition, rule adjustments, and output differences under parameter variation.

Taken together, the literature supports a clear engineering principle: transparency, controllability, and observability should be implemented as verifiable pipeline properties, not treated as interface decoration or after-the-fact commentary.

Design consequence: Chapter 3 should expose explicit controls, mechanism-linked explanation outputs, and run-level logging so that explanation fidelity and parameter sensitivity can be tested in Chapter 4.

## 2.3 Music Recommendation and Playlist-Specific Challenges
Music recommendation introduces design challenges that are not fully captured by generic top-$N$ item recommendation formulations [@schedl_current_2018; @deldjoo_content-driven_2024]. Listening behavior is often sequential, contextual, and tied to affective or situational factors. A user evaluating a playlist is not only judging isolated tracks, but also transitions, pacing, coherence, surprise, and overall listening flow. As a result, playlist generation should not be reduced to independent item relevance alone [@neto_algorithmic_2023; @gatzioura_hybrid_2019].

The playlist literature consistently highlights tensions among diversity, coherence, novelty, and ordering [@ferraro_automatic_2018; @vall_feature-combination_2019; @zamani_analysis_2019]. A highly coherent list may feel repetitive; a highly diverse list may break flow. Challenge analyses and implementation reports further show that outcomes depend on how candidate generation, feature handling, rank fusion, and continuation logic are composed under particular evaluation protocols [@zamani_analysis_2019; @teinemaa_composition_2018]. This is important for an artefact-based thesis because it supports a pipeline view of playlist generation: recommendation quality emerges from multiple stages and constraints, not from scoring alone.

Earlier synthesis on automated playlist continuation similarly warns that conclusions are shaped by protocol design, popularity effects, and method composition [@bonnin_automated_2015]. Related benchmark literature strengthens the same methodological caution: reported gains depend on the surrounding evaluation conditions, not only on the scoring algorithm [@mcfee_million_2012; @bauer_exploring_2024]. This supports a restrained claim discipline in later chapters. If the artefact performs well, the defensible conclusion is that the chosen deterministic design is viable under this thesis protocol, not that it dominates alternative recommender paradigms in the abstract.

Music recommendation also raises a representational challenge. Low-level computable signals do not map perfectly onto human musical meaning [@bogdanov_semantic_2013; @deldjoo_content-driven_2024]. Similarity is therefore not an objective truth that can be fully captured by a single metric. This is reinforced by music-similarity studies showing limits in agreement and strong dependence on framing and feature choice [@flexer_problem_2016; @siedenburg_modeling_2017]. In this thesis, deterministic similarity is treated as an auditable decision-support mechanism, not as a claim to universal musical correctness.

The canonical corpus choice is nonetheless literature-supported. Music4All offers a multi-signal music-information-retrieval resource with metadata, tags, lyrics, and audio features that are suitable for reproducible content-driven experimentation [@pegoraro_santana_music4all_2020]. Independent third-party usage strengthens its defensibility as a practical corpus for benchmark-informed experimentation, even where tasks are not identical to this thesis [@ru_improving_2023]. The claim here remains bounded: Music4All is suitable for the scoped artefact, not proven universally optimal for every playlist-generation task.

Finally, context matters. Music preference changes with mood, task, time, and setting [@schedl_current_2018; @andjelkovic_moodplay_2019]. The locked thesis scope does not attempt to model all such complexity. Instead, it treats context coverage as partial and prioritizes explicit, replayable preference signals over broad latent inference. That trade-off is defensible because it preserves observability and keeps the artefact within feasible scope.

Design consequence: Chapter 3 should model playlist generation as a staged process with explicit playlist-level constraints, bounded similarity interpretation, and a clearly justified canonical corpus.

## 2.4 Deterministic Feature-Based Design Rationale with Comparator Context
Feature-based recommendation remains attractive when the system must expose what inputs drove an output and how those inputs were combined [@deldjoo_content-driven_2024; @bogdanov_semantic_2013]. In an artefact whose contribution is centred on transparent, controllable, and observable engineering, feature-level representations provide a strong basis for inspectability. They enable explicit reporting of which attributes contributed to preference profiles, which similarity components drove candidate ranking, and how rule-based adjustments affected final playlist composition.

Deterministic design further strengthens that position. Under fixed configuration and fixed input, a deterministic pipeline should produce replayable outputs. This property is valuable not because determinism is always the best route to recommendation accuracy, but because it improves auditability, debugging clarity, and evidence quality. If identical inputs produce different outputs for hidden reasons, it becomes harder to evaluate the effect of controls, isolate bugs, or defend claims about explanation fidelity. Deterministic design therefore supports not only transparency but also observability and reproducibility [@zhang_explainable_2020; @tintarev_survey_2007].

At the same time, the literature does not support dismissing hybrid and neural methods. Hybrid recommenders can combine complementary information sources and often improve predictive performance [@cano_hybrid_2017; @he_neural_2017]. Recent multimodal and self-supervised work in music recommendation shows the same broader direction toward richer representation learning and competitive task performance [@liu_multimodal_2025; @yu_self_supervised_2024; @ru_improving_2023; @moysis_music_2023; @zhu_muq_2025]. These approaches matter as comparator context because they clarify the trade-off being made.

That trade-off is not simply simplicity versus sophistication. It is inspectability versus representational complexity under the thesis objectives. Richer models may capture patterns that a feature-based deterministic system misses, but they also tend to increase explanation difficulty, implementation burden, and evaluation ambiguity unless substantial interpretability infrastructure is added. For a BSc-scoped artefact whose contribution is engineering evidence rather than model novelty, deterministic feature-based scoring is therefore justified as a goal-aligned design choice.

Metric sensitivity is an important caveat within that choice. Similarity-measure research shows that distance definition can materially change system behavior [@fkih_similarity_2022]. Music-similarity research further suggests that no single metric consistently captures all relevant perceptual dimensions [@flexer_problem_2016; @siedenburg_modeling_2017]. Playlist-generation and benchmark studies reinforce that metric and protocol choices shape comparative conclusions [@bonnin_automated_2015; @zamani_analysis_2019]. Newer music-domain studies strengthen this point by showing that distance-function choice affects playlist coherence interpretation, although direct metric-swap evidence remains limited in breadth under playlist objectives [@furini_social_2024; @schweiger_impact_2025].

This means deterministic scoring should not hide metric selection inside implementation defaults. Metric choice, normalization decisions, and feature weighting must be surfaced as design decisions with stated rationale. The literature currently supports this requirement strongly enough for design justification, but it also leaves one bounded limitation: broad multi-dataset evidence isolating deterministic similarity-function effects across multiple playlist-objective outcomes remains limited in the present source set. That limitation should remain explicit rather than silently ignored.

On balance, the literature supports a conditional conclusion: deterministic feature-based recommendation is a defensible engineering basis for this artefact because it better supports explanation fidelity, explicit control, replayable behavior, and auditable evaluation under current scope. It is not advanced here as a universal accuracy-maximizing solution.

Design consequence: Chapter 3 should make feature selection, metric choice, weight configuration, and rule adjustments explicit so that Chapter 4 can test replayability, explanation fidelity, and parameter sensitivity.

## 2.5 Cross-Source Alignment, Reliability, and Reproducibility
The research question explicitly involves cross-source music preference data, which makes data alignment a central engineering concern rather than a peripheral preprocessing step. User listening histories and the candidate corpus originate from different systems, use partially different identifiers, and may vary in completeness or cleanliness. Entity-resolution literature strongly supports staged matching pipelines that combine blocking, candidate generation, and final matching instead of relying on a single fuzzy-match step [@elmagarmid_duplicate_2007; @allam_improved_2018; @papadakis_blocking_2021; @binette_almost_2022]. This staged structure improves computational feasibility and can improve linkage quality by narrowing comparison space before expensive or ambiguous decisions are made.

Within thesis scope, ISRC-first matching with metadata fallback is consistent with that literature. Exact identifier matching offers a high-precision route when identifiers are present and accurate, while metadata fallback can recover additional matches where identifiers are absent or inconsistent. This does not guarantee perfect linkage, but it provides a methodologically grounded and inspectable strategy for a deterministic artefact.

The alternative literature is still relevant. Neural entity-matching methods can improve difficult matching cases in noisier scenarios [@barlaug_neural_2021], but they also increase model complexity and reduce straightforward inspectability unless additional traceability mechanisms are added. For this artefact, the trade-off favors deterministic staged matching with explicit unmatched-case reporting over more opaque matching machinery.

The evidence base here must still be interpreted cautiously. Much of the strongest support for staged entity resolution is cross-domain rather than music-specific [@papadakis_blocking_2021; @allam_improved_2018; @elmagarmid_duplicate_2007]. That means the thesis can defend staged alignment as a principled engineering choice, but it should not overstate the precision of music-specific reliability expectations. Explicit unmatched-rate reporting, fallback-path accounting, and failure-case logging are therefore part of responsible claim discipline.

Reproducibility literature in recommender systems strengthens the same governance logic. Weak reporting of configurations, preprocessing choices, and evaluation conditions undermines accountability and makes comparison difficult [@beel_towards_2016; @bellogin_improving_2021; @cavenaghi_systematic_2023; @ferrari_dacrema_troubling_2021; @zhu_bars_2022; @anelli_elliot_2021; @betello_reproducible_2025]. For an artefact-based thesis, reproducibility is not optional implementation polish. It is part of the evidence that the artefact was engineered in a disciplined and critique-ready way.

Observability sits at the center of that requirement. A reproducible artefact should record enough context to explain what happened during a run: input summaries, alignment statistics, configuration settings, intermediate scoring traces, and final output artifacts. This is not merely for debugging convenience. It is necessary for thesis-level argumentation because later chapters must show not only that outputs were produced, but how they were produced and under what conditions they can be replayed.

Recent explainability work in music models reinforces the same principle at a different level: explanation is strongest when it exposes contribution structure rather than only aggregate results [@sotirou_musiclime_2025]. Even though this thesis does not implement deep multimodal explainers, the methodological implication transfers directly. Explanation, alignment diagnostics, and run logs should remain coupled to actual mechanism-level behavior.

Design consequence: Chapter 3 should implement staged alignment diagnostics, unmatched reporting, and run-level configuration logging so that uncertainty and replayability remain inspectable in Chapter 4.

## 2.6 Literature Gap, Synthesis, and Chapter Conclusion
The current literature provides strong support for individual ingredients relevant to this thesis: explainable recommendation, controllability studies, playlist-generation constraints, content-based music recommendation, entity-resolution design, and reproducibility governance. What is less fully developed in the present source set is practical end-to-end guidance for engineering one coherent artefact that combines all of these concerns under explicit transparency, controllability, and observability requirements.

That gap matters because this thesis does not claim a novel recommender model. Its contribution lies in artefact engineering knowledge: how to build a deterministic playlist-generation pipeline that remains inspectable, controllable, observable, and reproducible while operating on cross-source music preference data. The literature supports the need for such a contribution by showing that each constituent concern is important, but it does not collapse them into one ready-made architectural blueprint. The design work in subsequent chapters is therefore justified as synthesis and operationalization rather than invention without precedent.

The review supports a coherent set of design consequences. Method selection should be goal-aligned rather than benchmark-driven in isolation [@roy_systematic_2022; @jannach_measuring_2019]. Transparency claims are stronger when explanations remain coupled to actual score contributors and rule adjustments [@zhang_explainable_2020; @tintarev_evaluating_2012]. Controllability should be demonstrated through interpretable parameter effects rather than asserted abstractly [@jin_effects_2020; @nauta_anecdotal_2023]. Music recommendation requires explicit attention to playlist-level sequencing, diversity, and coherence rather than item-level relevance alone [@schedl_current_2018; @neto_algorithmic_2023; @vall_feature-combination_2019]. Cross-source alignment is best treated as a staged and diagnosable entity-resolution problem with bounded uncertainty [@papadakis_blocking_2021; @allam_improved_2018]. Reproducibility and observability function as evidence-quality requirements, not optional implementation conveniences [@beel_towards_2016; @anelli_elliot_2021; @betello_reproducible_2025].

Residual limitations remain explicit. The literature base is stronger on domain-general entity resolution than on music-specific alignment benchmarks, and stronger on the principle of metric sensitivity than on broad isolation studies for deterministic similarity functions across multiple playlist-objective settings. These are evidence-depth limits, not blockers to the thesis contribution, but they should remain visible in later interpretation.

Overall, the chapter establishes a defensible literature basis for the revised research question. It frames the thesis as an artefact-based investigation into how a transparent, controllable, and observable playlist-generation pipeline can be engineered under constrained scope. It also places clear boundaries on what later claims may assert: if the artefact succeeds, the defensible contribution is scope-specific engineering viability and evaluable design evidence, not universal superiority over hybrid or neural recommenders.

Chapter transition: Chapter 3 should translate these literature-supported considerations into concrete architecture decisions covering preference construction, candidate filtering, deterministic scoring, playlist assembly, explanation artifacts, staged alignment diagnostics, and run-level observability controls.
