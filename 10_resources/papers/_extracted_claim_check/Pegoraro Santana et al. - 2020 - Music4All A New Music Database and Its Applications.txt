INTRODUCTION Music goes beyond what we know as an element for fun or distraction, as it plays an important role on education and development of the human being in society [1]. It is a social phenomenon capable of maintaining traditional roles in different societies, even over the centuries. Through music, children can change how they think, act and establish relationships with friends transposing the idea of a simple song when raising its level to an element of social formation [2]. Music information retrieval (MIR) is a research area that has been rapidly gaining attention due to the widespread digital distribution of music. Examples of such distribution are commercial applications like Spotifyl, Apple Music2 and Pandora3 . In 2017, the commercialization of digital media presented a rise in revenue of 41.1%, while the revenue from the physical media dropped 5.4%. This growth represents a profit of 9 billion dollars for the sector, the highest recorded profit until now [3]. These numbers emphasize the relevance of the area and the many opportunities in it. One of the goals of MIR is to research new methods and create new systems that can efficiently and effectively retrieve and recommend songs from large databases of music content. However, there is a lack of music databases to support this kind of research. Although several different databases have already been presented in the literature, there is still a lack of databases that comply with some quite desirable requirements for the development of researches, such as: a huge amount of music pieces, the audio signal availability and a great diversity of audio attributes. Aiming at contributing to the MIR community, in this paper we present Music4All, a new music database which contains metadata, tags, genre information, audio clips, lyrics, and so on which are essentials for the research and development of new digital music systems. THE DATABASE Music4all was designed to be a useful database in different domains of research. Therefore, it needed to gather different types of informa­ tion related to the songs. To achieve this purpose, the development process was split into two phases, called the user phase and the music phase. In the user phase we gather information about the listening history of users, and in the song phase we collected data about the songs (i.e. audio, lyrics, etc). Figure 1 presents a summary of the entire database development process. The steps of the two phases are represented by rectangles and the data sources from which the data were retrieved are represented by out-of-rectangles database icons. The steps that contain a filter icon on the top-right are steps in which data was removed (filtered) from the original database. Filtering steps were performed to prevent songs with missing information from being kept in the database. The user phase was initiated by sampling a random lastfm user as a starting point. From this user, we gathered all of its friends through the lastfm API and recursively obtained the circle of friends, excluding duplicates. We collected 40,940 users in this step. For all retrieved users, we collected their listening histories ranging from January 1st, 2019 00:00 to March 20th, 2019 23:59. We delimited this range of dates to obtain recent, popular and well-known songs. In the following step, we anonymized the database by discarding any personal information and metadata collected through the lastfm API5, including the relationship between users. We generated a new randomly identifier for each user. Finally, to remove outliers (users that did not have much songs) and produce a denser database, we used a threshold to filter the users and keep only the ones that had at least 1,000 plays in their listening histories. Users that did not meet this threshold were discarded, resulting in 15,602 users and 320,073 songs at the end of the user phase. The song phase consisted of collecting data from songs, which included tags, lyrics, genres, audio, and metadata. We retrieved this data from different sources and merged it into the database. Again, to avoid keeping songs with missing information, we discarded the ones for which we were unable to retrieve all necessary data. Tags for each song were retrieved by a lastfm crawler that accessed each song's page. We decided to use a crawler because it can return more tags than the API. The main goal of a Music Recommender System is to propose to a given user interesting songs to listen to, as well as unknown artists and their tracks, based on the personal preference from the user [4]. One of the applications in which our database can be applied, in this research area, is suggesting playlists to the user, and this subsection focuses on this application. As the database contains the listening history for each user, and the timestamp in which each song was listened by the user; the listening history for each user was rearranged into sessions. To split into sessions, songs that were listened by the same user 60 minutes apart from each other were placed in different sessions. To represent the songs and capture the intrinsic features of the sequence in which they were listened by a given user, we used the two models proposed by [5]: music2vec and session-music2vec. Those models were built using as a basis the word2vec model [6]. The difference between those models is that music2vec infers the user's preference from the entirety of its listening history, with the intent of learning the general preference from music, while session-music2vec infers the contextual preference from the current session only (i.e. the context of the listening), focusing on the current interest of the user. Both preferences are calculated by the mean average of the vector representations of the songs in the listening history and the current session. These preferences are used by the recommendation algorithms. The session-based algorithms used to suggest playlists were the ones proposed by [5]: Music2vec-TopN (M-TN), SessionMusic2vec-TopN (SM-TN), Context-Session-Music2vec-TopN(CSM-TN) and Context­ Session-Music2vec-UserKNN (CSM-UK). The M-TN algorithm determines the preference between a user and a song by the cosine similarity between the user's preference and the song embeddings obtained by music2vec. The SM-TN is similar to the M-TN algorithm, but it uses the contextual preference and the song embeddings obtained by session-music2vec. III. Mood Classification Applications such as retrieving music by genre or emotion have stood out in the MIR area. In particular, Music Emotion Recognition (MER) has received increasing attention from the scientific commu­ nity, as emotion is a fundamental attribute from a song [20]. Several works in the literature propose automatic methods for MER. The most common way to approach this application is to categorize songs using specific emotions labels, such as happy, sad, calm, angry [21]-[23]. Other way to approach the problem is by representing emotion, or aspects related to it, as continuous values [24], [25]. The songs in our database contain continuous values of valence (polarity) and energy (arousal), which in turn can be used for emotion recognition, so we have adopted the second approach. Although there is more information available in the database, it is fairly common to find works [22], [26], [27] that use only lyrics for mood prediction. Therefore, we used only lyrics on this application. We used two Long Short-Term Memory (LSTM) model, one to infer valence values and one to infer energy values. Both are fairly simple LSTM model with three layers: an embedding layer, which learns a representation of the word in real-valued vector; a LSTM layer, which learns intrinsic features from the input data; and a dense layer, that maps the features learnt by the LSTM to a final prediction. The LSTM layer had 128 units, and the data was fed to the model with a batch size of 2.048 in 100 epochs. Finally, we used the sigmoid as activation function for the dense layer. Automatic music genre classification is an important task in MIR, and several works approach this task with different techniques and data sources [9], [10]. By knowing that a song contains different data, such as lyrics, audio, video clip, and metadata, and that each one of those has different characteristics of the same song, some data will be more suitable to a certain task than to others. When performing music genre classification, audio-based approaches receives a significant amount of attention from the MIR community [11], showing good results and presenting the audio as an important data source for this task. Due to this fact, we also used the audio from the songs in this application. Several features can be used in audio-based music genre classi­ fication, from traditional features like Mel-frequency cepstral coeffi­ cients (MFCCs) [12] and Statistical Spectrum Descriptors (SSDs) [13] (extracted from the raw audio) to representation learning features (e.g. Convolutional Neural Networks (CNNs». Uke in other machine learning tasks, deep learning approaches such as CNNs have overcome the results of traditional techniques and achieved state-of-the-art results in music genre classification as well. Most of the work in the music genre classification area approaches a multi-class scenario, where each music has only one genre. But, in a real-world application, music usually has more than one genre tag associated to it, characterizing a multi-label classification problem. In a multi-label problem, each instance is assigned to one or more labels [14]. In this work, we performed multi-label genre classification using CNN with the spectrograms generated from the audio clips. The CNN architecture used was the Inception_v3 [15], a robust CNN model that has achieved good results in various classification tasks. The experimental protocol was performed with 5-fold cross­ validation by using artist filtering [16] with a minimum of 100 music pieces per genre in each fold, resulting in a subset with 65 genres tags and 103,568 songs. To run the experiments, we used the 30-seconds audio clip provided by the database and extracted its spectrogram representation using SoX library [17]. We have used the Inception_v3 [15] with its pre-trained weights using the ImageNet Dataset [18]. After the convolutional layers, which gives us the feature maps, it has two dense layers with 2,048 nodes that use ReLU [19] as the activation function. Then, we have used the sigmoid as the activation function in the classification layer because its a multi-label classification problem. This layer was set to have 65 nodes to match the number of genres in our subset. The results obtained with the Inception_v3 are presented in Table III. In this table, if we take into account metrics that consider only correct predictions, the results are 0.2786 for the Accuracy and 0.3703 for the F-measure. RELATED WORKS In this section we describe some of the most important music databases used by the research community to support the development of works on MIR. We briefly remark some historical facts about these databases, and other details about them, as the number of music pieces available, audio signal availability, number of class labels and so on. The databases are presented predominantly in chronological order. RWC is one of the first databases presented to the MIR research community. It is a copyright-cleared music database, originally made available as a common foundation for the research development. RWC is the acronym for Real World Computing, and it was created by the RWC Music Database Sub-Working Group of the Real World Computing Partnership (RWCP) of Japan. Although the RWC database has shown to be quite useful for a while, it is undeniable that its small number of samples (i.e. only one hundred of music pieces) imposed limitations to address the new challenges which have emerged. GTZAN is among the most traditional music databases used by the MIR research community for investigations on the genre classification task. This database was presented by Tzanetakis and Cook [28], who were also the first to treat music genre classification as a pattern recognition task. In addition to the database, the authors also presented baseline results, which encouraged other researchers to use the same database for comparison purposes. The database is balanced regarding the number of samples per class, there are one hundred music pieces classified in ten genres, namely Blues, Classical, Country, Disco, Hip Hop, Jazz, Metal, Popular, Reggae, and Rock. Berenzweig et al. [29] presented the USPOP 2002 database, com­ posed of 8,752 music pieces assigned to ten genres. The authors made available the full content of the songs under request, to ensure the use for research purposes. Originally created to support the development of the ISMIR 2004 Audio Description Contest, ISMIR 2004 has become one of the most widely used databases for music genre classification because it was chosen as one of the databases to be explored in the genre classification task in several editions of the annual Music Information Retrieval Evaluation eXchange (MIREX), a contest that happens as part of the conference of the International Society for Music Information Retrieval (ISMIR). Although it was created and launched in 2004, it was described in details in the Technical Report of the Music Technology Group of the University Pompeu Fabra [30] in 2006. The database provides the full audio content of 1,458 music pieces, labeled according to one of the following six classes: Classical, Electronic, Jazz/Blues, Metal/Punk, RockIPop, and World. Homburg et al. presented in [31] a new database, called HOMBURG set, created for MIR research. The database is composed of 1,886 music title assigned to nine different music genres, namely Blues, Electronic, Jazz, Pop, Rap/HipHop, Rock, Folk/Country, Alternative, and Funk/Soul. The use of the database remained limited because only 10 seconds of signal of each music was provided. Gouyon et al. [32] performed experiments on a ballroom database collected from BallroomDancers.com ll . The database is composed of 698 instances from eight dancing styles. The audio clips provided have 30-seconds duration. Ilhttp://www.ballroomdancers.com 403 The Computer Audition Lab 500 (CAL500) database is made up of 500 Western popular song excerpts of different lengths [33]. The excerpts were annotated with a set of 174 different tags. Holzapfel and Stylianou [34] used a dance styles database that contains music clips assigned to six classes of dances commonly en­ countered in the island of Crete: Kalamatianos, Siganos, Maleviziotis, (fast) Pentozalis, Sousta and Chaniotis. Each class contains either fifteen or sixteen instrumental song excerpts of about ten seconds length. It is important to observe that, as in the case of the ballroom database previously described, this kind of classes are more suitable for investigation about "tempo induction" than "music genre", as evidenced in the "tempo induction" contest occurred as part of the annual ISMIR conference in 2004. The 1517-artists databasel2 is composed of 3,180 music titles assigned to nineteen labels. It was organized by Seyerlehner et al. [35], and the songs are available under request. The Latin Music Database (LMD) was presented by Silla et al. [36] in 2008. The database has become one of the databases used in the genre contest in the annual ISMIR conference. The most important remark about LMD is that it is a database composed of Latin music genres, quite different from traditional western genres, typically provided in the other widely used databases. The database is composed of 3,227 samples from ten different genres, namely Axe, Pagode, Bachata, Bolero, Forr6, GaJicha, Merengue, Salsa, Sertanejo, and Tango. Despite of the new challenges provided by this database, its use is limited by the fact that the audio content itself was not made available, due to copyright restrictions. The Magnatagatune database consists of 21,642 excerpts of length 30 seconds from 230 different artists collected by using the TagATune game [37]. Excerpts annotations are among a set of 188 tags. The Million Songs Dataset (MSD) is the biggest database already created for MIR research purposes [38]. Despite this remarkable characteristic, the music content is not publicly available, therefore limiting its use. The database is composed of one million music titles, and it was launched in 2011 as a collaborative project between The Echo Nest and LabROSA13 , from the Columbia University. CONCLUSION AND FUTURE WORK In this paper we presented Music4All, a new music database which contains metadata, tags, genre information, 30-seconds audio clips, lyrics, and so on. We started by describing in detail how the database was created. Additionally, we also presented some examples of MIR applications that may be developed by using our database. Finally, we compared it against other databases proposed in the literature. Music4All was designed to be a database useful in distinct domains of research, and as future work we intent to explore it in many different applications. It is noteworthy that although we have exemplified the use of the database with applications that used only one of the types of information available, the database is also suitable for research involving multimodal applications. Thus, future work will also involve research focused on multimodal applications.
