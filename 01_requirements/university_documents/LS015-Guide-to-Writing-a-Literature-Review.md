# Guide to Writing a Literature Review

## What is a literature review?

A literature review is a comprehensive review of the literature available for a given research question. It is a summary, analysis, and evaluation of the literature and an explanation of what research has already been performed for a research area.

## Why do a literature review?

A literature review helps you:

- show what research has already been done on a subject
- highlight areas that may have had less research carried out on them
- justify a research proposal
- identify areas where further research would be useful
- discuss the theories and concepts that underpin your research
- explain terminology and define less obvious concepts
- critically analyse the material you are reviewing

## Beginning your literature review

1. Choose a topic or research question.
2. Analyse it and think of search terms, synonyms, and alternate spellings.
3. Formulate a search strategy.
4. Keep a record of the searches you perform.
5. Review the quantity and quality of the results and narrow or broaden the search if needed.
6. Assess whether the material is suitable and credible.

If you find too little material, reconsider the angle or scope of the question. Limited literature may reflect ethical or technical barriers in the area.

## Writing your literature review

Once you have identified credible, current, and accurate sources, group the information into subtopics or themes.

You should:

- describe your subject
- demonstrate your understanding
- explain what research has been done
- discuss how the literature affects your own research
- use quotations where appropriate without over-relying on them
- reference fully to avoid plagiarism

## Structure of a literature review

A literature review should usually contain:

- **Introduction**: defines the research area and any exclusions.
- **Main body**: organized around themes or subtopics identified from the research.
- **Conclusion**: summarizes the findings and justifies the chosen research topic without introducing new information.
- **Reference list / bibliography**: all sources used or consulted.

The Wolverhampton Harvard style should be used unless another standard is specified.
