# What is a literature review?

A literature review is:

- a comprehensive review of the literature available for a research area or question
- a summary, analysis, and critical evaluation of that literature
- an explanation of what research has already been performed in the area

# Why do a literature review?

A literature review helps you:

- collect, study, compare, and evaluate current knowledge
- demonstrate your understanding of the field
- identify what research has already been done
- highlight less-researched areas
- build the background and context needed to create something new

# When do a literature review?

A literature review may:

- be a stand-alone project
- form part of a larger dissertation or project
- start before the research question is fully posed
- continue throughout the project

<!-- Slide number: 10 -->
# Types of literature review

- **Narrative review**: comprehensive but does not necessarily make search and inclusion rules explicit.
- **Systematic review**: follows explicit rules for searching, analysing, and synthesising studies.

<!-- Slide number: 11 -->
# What is included?

Possible content includes:

- historical background
- current research context
- questions, debates, and significance
- related work
- limitations
- relevant theories and concepts
- disagreements and alternative perspectives
- key papers and thinkers
- definitions of relevant terms
- gaps in the literature
- calls for further research

<!-- Slide number: 12 -->
# General structure of a literature review

- Introduction: define the area, scope, exclusions, and organization.
- Main sections: connect your question or aims to the literature, show coherence, identify major thinkers and disagreements, compare and contrast authors.

The lecture reminds students to write in the third person.

# Also think about paragraph structure

Paragraphs should:

- introduce a theme or topic
- position research findings
- elaborate with supporting detail
- state the significance of findings

<!-- Slide number: 16 -->
# Your line of argument

Make the argument easy to follow by:

- starting a new paragraph for each new idea
- using signposting for additions, for example "Moreover" or "Furthermore"
- using signposting for contrasts, for example "However" or "In contrast"

<!-- Slide number: 17 -->
# Critical writing

Be analytical rather than descriptive. Be clear and concise, provide evidence and reasoning, and support your own ideas with sources.

# Synthesis

Synthesis:

- combines ideas from several sources
- focuses on connections and differences
- goes beyond summary
- relates evidence back to the question or area
- generates new ideas beyond the source text

# Paraphrasing

Suggested steps:

1. Fully understand the source text.
2. Think about why you are using the source.
3. Select the elements that support your work.
4. Put the source into your own words and voice.
