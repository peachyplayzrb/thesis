# Artefact testing

Artefact testing evaluates the artefact against the academic question and aims. This is different from functional testing, which should generally not be described in detail in the academic report.

<!-- Slide number: 4 -->
# Artefact testing

What you do and the type of data you collect depends on the project. For example, a web security project may need security testing.

<!-- Slide number: 5 -->
# Artefact testing

Three general steps:

1. Gather data.
2. Analyse the data.
3. Evaluate the results of the analysis.

Possible data-gathering methods include tests, experiments, questionnaires, and semi-structured interviews.

<!-- Slide number: 6 -->
# Artefact testing and methodology

How you perform these steps generally defines your project's research methodology.

<!-- Slide number: 7 -->
# Research methodology

Research methodology may involve:

- Gathering data
- Potentially formulating a hypothesis or proposition
- Testing the hypothesis or academic question
- Analysing the data
- Evaluating the results

<!-- Slide number: 8 -->
# Hypothesis

A research question can sometimes be refined into a hypothesis, although not all projects require one. A hypothesis is a short statement about the relationship between variables that can be tested through experimentation.

<!-- Slide number: 9 -->
# Research methodology - types of data

Two broad types of data:

- Qualitative data: opinions, textual feedback, descriptions
- Quantitative data: measurable numeric data such as time, counts, or visits

# Quantitative data

Quantitative data tells you what is happening, for example:

- This screen is slower than that one.
- Visitors stay only 5 seconds on a page.
- Customers spend less on a new site.

It may be collected automatically or through scaled feedback questions.

<!-- Slide number: 12 -->
# Qualitative data

Qualitative data looks at why something is happening.

Methods may include:

- Heuristic analysis
- User surveys and interviews
- Think-aloud methodologies

<!-- Slide number: 13 -->
# Design

Possible research designs mentioned include:

- Surveys and questionnaires
- A/B testing
- Simulation data
- Structured interviews
- Unstructured interviews
- Semi-structured interviews

<!-- Slide number: 14 -->
# Questionnaires

Questionnaires can be useful but badly designed questionnaires can produce unusable data.

Tips:

- Be clear about what you want to know.
- Decide who to ask.
- Keep the questionnaire clear and well presented.

<!-- Slide number: 15 -->
# Questionnaires

Question types:

- Open questions
- Closed questions

Guidance:

- Minimise bias.
- Avoid leading questions.
- Be specific and avoid jargon.
- Think carefully about the response options.
- Consider survey scales such as a 5-point or 7-point Likert scale.
