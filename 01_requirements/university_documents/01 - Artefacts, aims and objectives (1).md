# Academic question

Why do I need an academic question?

An academic question enables you to think clearly about your project, define the goals you want to reach, and keep focus.

Examples:

- "What are the main challenges involved in developing an algorithm to detect facial expressions?"
- "What are the main security threats to be considered when developing a web application?"

<!-- Slide number: 4 -->
# Aims of the project

The main aim of the project is to answer an academic question, but you should include 2-3 related aims.

An aim is a short description of what you intend to do or achieve on the project. Aims should be related both to the project and to the academic question.

<!-- Slide number: 5 -->
# Aims of the project - example

Academic question: "What are the main security threats to be considered when developing a web application?"

Aims:

- To investigate the main threats and possible solutions.
- To implement a complex, database-driven web application.
- To perform security testing using appropriate tools and methodology.

<!-- Slide number: 6 -->
# Objectives

Objectives are derived from the aims, but are more specific and should be measurable. They show how the aims will be accomplished.

Set SMART objectives that are:

- Specific
- Measurable
- Achievable
- Realistic
- Timed

<!-- Slide number: 7 -->
# Objectives - example

Aims and related objectives should be clearly linked. The lecture emphasizes converting broad aims into specific SMART tasks.

# Artefact

Common questions:

- What should my artefact be?
- How complex does it need to be?

Your artefact should have a sufficient level of challenge and be related to your aims and objectives.

<!-- Slide number: 9 -->
# Artefact - level of challenge

Project marking takes account of the level of challenge. This helps distinguish easier projects done very well from more difficult projects completed to a lower standard.

Typical factors:

- Problems overcome.
- Whether information was readily available.
- Breadth and depth of the project.
- Demonstration quality.

<!-- Slide number: 10 -->
# Artefact - level of challenge

Low-level challenge projects typically involve readily available information, fully known parts, fewer tools, and simpler technologies.

High-level challenge projects may involve real or industry-led problems, unknown direction, information that is not readily available, and a wider range of tools and technologies.

<!-- Slide number: 11 -->
# Artefact - related to aims

The artefact should help answer the academic question. For example, a web app security project needs an artefact that actually contains security-relevant features such as forms, accounts, and a database.

# Academic Misconduct

Forms of academic misconduct include:

- Plagiarism
- Collusion
- Inappropriate use of AI

Penalties range from failure of an assessment to failure of the course.

<!-- Slide number: 14 -->
# Plagiarism

Plagiarism is the use of someone else's work without attribution. This includes copying text or code from the internet or from another student.

To avoid plagiarism, give proper credit through referencing.

<!-- Slide number: 15 -->
# Plagiarism

All projects are submitted to Turnitin, which checks work against web pages, reports, papers, and publications.

<!-- Slide number: 16 -->
# Collusion

Collusion is when two or more people produce work and each submits it as their own. Exchanging ideas is allowed, but the actual work and final write-up must be your own.

<!-- Slide number: 17 -->
# AI - what is acceptable

Acceptable uses include:

- Initial research and idea generation.
- Improving writing.
- Clarifying complex concepts.

<!-- Slide number: 18 -->
# AI - what is not acceptable

Unacceptable uses include:

- Submitting AI-generated text as your own.
- Relying on AI for critical analysis.
- Using AI to falsify or fabricate data.

<!-- Slide number: 19 -->
# Cheating has consequences

The University treats plagiarism as a very serious offence. Detection methods include continuous monitoring, presentations or vivas, and Turnitin.
